In recent years, societies and individuals alike have been forced to navigate an increasingly complex web of external shocks. These disruptions—ranging from regional conflicts and the lingering effects of the global pandemic to rapid technological change—have been compounded by the intensifying consequences of climate change. These developments are reshaping not only how communities adapt to risk, but also how financial decision-makers grapple with a long-standing tension: how to create value in the traditional financial sense—returns, shareholder wealth, measurable performance—while also incorporating values such as climate responsibility, social equity, and long-term sustainability. That tension, once peripheral, now lies at the center of global financial debates.
In 2018, CEO Larry Fink’s annual letter to corporate leaders marked a watershed moment, urging companies to articulate a “sense of purpose” and demonstrate their contributions to society as a prerequisite for long-term profitability. This argument signaled a departure from shareholder primacy and encouraged companies to consider the broader societal implications of their operations.
By 2020, BlackRock moved from rhetoric to strategy, incorporating ESG (Environmental, Social, and Governance) factors across all active portfolios, distancing itself from thermal coal, and positioning climate risk explicitly as investment risk. The surge in ESG-labelled funds and the firm’s increased support for climate- and social-related shareholder proposals cemented its role as a global leader in sustainable investing.
The landscape shifted between 2022 and 2023. BlackRock’s ESG funds experienced significant outflows, driven by underperformance and a surge of anti-ESG sentiment. In response, Larry Fink abandon the term “ESG,” emphasizing instead BlackRock’s fiduciary duty to clients and its role in providing comprehensive risk information, including climate-related risks. The firm maintained its stance that climate risk remains investment risk but sought to balance its approach by continuing investments in fossil fuel companies while encouraging energy transition strategies.  This nuanced position reflects the complexities of navigating stakeholder expectations, regulatory pressures, and market realities.
The evolution of BlackRock’s messaging continued in subsequent years. In the 2024 Chairman’s Letter, Fink replaced “ESG” with terms such as stakeholder capitalism and energy pragmatism, highlighting the need to balance the transition to renewable energy with concerns about energy security.  
Building on the EC’s definition of sustainable finance, it introduces the core ESG dimensions. Environmental considerations encompass mitigation and adaptation to climate change, biodiversity preservation, pollution prevention, resource efficiency, and circularity. Social factors span human and animal rights, equity and inclusion, labor relations, and consumer protection. Governance addresses how public and private organizations are structured and run—board composition and oversight, incentive design and executive pay, internal controls, and stakeholder engagement.
In the 2020s, ESG considerations became central to financial regulation and corporate strategy across major economies. Disclosure frameworks such as GRI, TCFD, CSRD, and ISSB began shaping policy, and companies increasingly addressed issues beyond the environment, including labor standards and diversity. ESG matured into a multidimensional approach to value creation. These developments reflected a shift from shareholder primacy to stakeholder responsibility, embedding sustainability into the core of business legitimacy.
Despite its rapid adoption, ESG has faced growing criticism and skepticism. Since 2023, the field has experienced policy reversals, capital outflows, and increased scrutiny. In the United States, ESG has become a politically charged topic, with some critics arguing that it prioritizes social agendas over financial returns and distorts market mechanisms. In Europe, the energy crisis following the Ukraine conflict reignited debates about the exclusion of traditional energy from ESG portfolios, with fiscal pressures leading to a more pragmatic approach to climate policy.
These tensions have had tangible effects on capital flows. For example, global sustainable funds saw significant net outflows in the third quarter of 2025, with notable redemptions from UK-based BlackRock funds. Even after accounting for these large movements, sustainable investments overall experienced a reversal from previous inflows. This suggests that skepticism about ESG is spreading beyond the United States to other regions.
The controversy surrounding ESG stems from several factors. First, the reason, perhaps, lies not in ideology but in finance itself. Early outperformance was often driven by favorable market trends rather than inherent advantages. By 2022, however, the surge in fossil fuel prices after the Ukraine conflict benefited sectors typically excluded from ESG portfolios, while technology stocks—often overweight in ESG funds—underperformed.
Policy debates have also played a role. In the US, some lawmakers argue that excluding fossil fuels threatens energy security and jobs, while in Europe, there is pushback against regulations seen as too restrictive. ESG-focused financial institutions and climate advocates have recently faced prominent antitrust allegations. Critics claim that these groups have coordinated efforts—such as shareholder activism and selective capital allocation—to pressure companies into reducing activities that generate high emissions. Some argue that such collective actions may limit competition or unfairly influence business decisions.
Additionally, ESG has sometimes become more of a marketing tool or compliance exercise than a genuine driver of long-term value. The complexity and cost of meeting diverse stakeholder expectations have led to overpromised targets and, in some cases, accusations of “greenwashing”.
The evolution of sustainable finance reflects a journey from early environmental awareness to a complex, multidimensional approach to value creation. While ESG principles have become central to global finance, recent backlash highlights unresolved tensions between financial returns, regulatory expectations, and broader societal goals. Maintaining an objective perspective is essential as the field continues to adapt and redefine its purpose.
In today’s climate change and shifting policies, organizations and governments face a crucial decision: should they scale back ESG initiatives or reaffirm their commitment to sustainability? This dilemma reflects a broader tension between immediate economic pressures and the long-term risks that sustainability frameworks are designed to address.
In a world facing increasing uncertainty, climate risks, and technological change, reaffirming sustainable finance is both timely and meaningful. We hope this book provides valuable insights and prompts reflection on how sustainable finance relates to your academic interests or professional goals. Our purpose is to guide you in understanding, questioning, and applying sustainable finance in practice. 
Generally speaking, there are four main components in sustainability (ESG) ecosystem. As we can see in Figure 2.1, Companies are positioned at the upstream end of the ESG data flow, serving as the primary source of most sustainability information that investors focus on—such as carbon emissions, employee diversity, and anti-bribery measures. They release this information either voluntarily or to meet mandatory regulatory demands in their regions, following established sustainability standards or frameworks, thus producing ESG raw data. The raw data is collected by data aggregators like Bloomberg and LSEG, which calculate commonly used ESG metrics (e.g., carbon emission intensity or green revenue percentage), and display them consistently on their platforms. Meanwhile, ESG rating agencies like MSCI and Sustainalytics also leverage this raw data alongside additional sources (e.g., news releases and expert interviews). Using their own rating methodologies, these agencies conduct thorough, systematic assessments of companies’ sustainability performance across sectors, providing standardized ratings to enable cross-company comparisons. The end users of the ESG data flow are banks or investors, who integrate these ESG metrics or ratings into their investment or risk management processes. For example, they might adjust loan interest rates based on a company’s ESG rating or use the carbon intensity of invested companies to shape engagement strategies or divestment decisions. 
According to the above illustration, it is clear that calculating sustainability metrics from ESG raw data and directly referencing ESG ratings represent unstructured and structured approaches, respectively, to evaluating a company’s ESG performance. The second part will focus on introducing key ESG rating agencies and explaining the core methodologies analysts employ to rate companies. 
Sustainability standards or frameworks guide companies in disclosing ESG information to investors and a broader range of stakeholders, enabling businesses to communicate their impact. The sustainability reports generated through these standards or frameworks serve as a vital resource for stakeholders seeking to understand a company’s ESG and climate policies. Over the past two decades, numerous organizations have established various types of sustainability disclosure standards or frameworks to regulate corporate ESG reporting. Each differs in its scope and depth of sustainability topics, materiality assessment requirements, and intended audience. Impact Materiality, in contrast, focuses on the effects of a company’s activities on the economy, environment, and society. This includes aspects like greenhouse gas emissions, water usage, waste management, labor rights, or community impacts. For instance, a business might report on its efforts to reduce its carbon footprint or improve working conditions in its supply chain. The emphasis is on the company’s responsibility to external stakeholders and its contribution to sustainable development, highlighting its broader societal and environmental footprint.
Conducting a materiality assessment to identify material topics and subsequently reporting on a company’s performance, governance, strategy, and targets related to these topics forms the core of nearly all sustainability reporting. In practice, every type of standards mentions the interplay between a company and its external environment to some extent, though each has a distinct focus. Standards or frameworks emphasizing financial materiality—such as IIRC, SASB, ISSB, CDSB, TCFD, and TNFD—primarily target investors as their audience. Those focusing on impact materiality, like GRI, cater to a broader group of stakeholders, including regulators and communities. Meanwhile, ESRS and CDP balance both perspectives, prioritizing double materiality.
As the field of sustainability disclosure evolves, a broad range of stakeholders, including investors, increasingly demand a consistent and integrated global sustainability disclosure system. This will allow companies to reduce compliance burdens and generate comparable—even verifiable—sustainability reports across industries, which will enhance the quality of ESG raw data, making it more actionable for investors. As illustrated in the table above, various standards and frameworks are not in isolated competition. In fact, since the TCFD’s release in 2017, these disclosure systems have begun to reference, align with, and even merge with one another: In 2018, CDP redesigned its climate change questionnaire to align with the TCFD recommendations. In April of the same year, CDSB officially announced on its website that its reporting framework matched TCFD structure. In 2021, SASB and IIRC officially announced their merger to form the Value Reporting Foundation, and in 2022, they merged with CDSB into the IFRS Foundation. In 2023, TCFD announced its dissolution and transferred all its functions to ISSB. The IFRS S1 and S2 sustainability and climate disclosure standards released by ISSB in the same year fully adopted the 4 disclosure pillars and 11 recommended disclosures of TCFD and provided detailed guidance. Moreover, TNFD and the EU’s ESRS disclosure standard E1—Climate Change, released in 2023, have also adopted or integrated the TCFD framework.
We can observe that countries in Asia vary significantly in both the progress and the standards of their sustainability disclosure requirements. This divergence raises several important questions for further discussion. For instance, will the ISSB standards eventually play a role similar to the IFRS accounting standards, serving as a “license to operate” in global capital markets? From the corporate perspective, how should firms weigh the trade-off between compliance costs and the benefits of market recognition? This dilemma may be especially pronounced for multinational corporations that must navigate and reconcile different ESG disclosure requirements across Asian jurisdictions.

In short, rating agencies assess a company’s sustainability performance across the three pillars of environment, social, and governance, with each pillar encompassing specific sustainability topics of interest. Taking LSEG as an example, it focuses on emissions, innovation, and resource use under the Environmental pillar; human rights, product responsibility, workforce, and community under the Social pillar; and management, shareholders, and CSR strategy under the Governance pillar. Each topic is broken down into key issues. For instance, when evaluating the workforce topic, issues such as diversity and inclusion, career development and training, working conditions, and health and safety are prioritized. To measure these key issues, specific qualitative or quantitative metrics are established, and data is collected. Thus, the ESG composite score, pillar scores, topic scores (sometimes we also call it theme scores), key issue scores, and underlying metrics (based on data points) form the five levels at which rating agencies evaluate a company’s sustainability performance. A company’s financing activities form the foundational step for undertaking various projects. The core objective of financing is to secure the necessary funds to achieve strategic and operational goals. Broadly, financing can be divided into two categories: equity financing and debt financing. Equity financing involves raising funds by selling company shares, allowing investors to become shareholders and share in the company’s profits and growth potential, though it dilutes existing shareholders’ ownership. For instance, in 2022, Chinese wind power company Sany Renewable Energy conducted an initial public offering, raising nearly 5.5 billion RMB to fund production line upgrades, turbine R&D, and wind power industrial park projects. Debt financing, on the other hand, involves raising funds through issuing bonds or borrowing from financial institutions, requiring the company to repay the principal and interest within an agreed timeframe. For example, in 2022, Danish turbine manufacturer Vestas secured a 475 million EUR green loan from the European Investment Bank to support R&D for large-megawatt turbines. The choice between these financing methods depends not only on a company’s financial health, market conditions, and industry characteristics but also on external factors such as macroeconomic policies, financial market volatility, and investor preferences. In recent years, as concepts like sustainability and climate change have gained prominence, regulators and financial systems worldwide have increasingly integrated companies’ ESG performance into regulatory and investment decisions. Consequently, a company’s sustainability performance has become a key factor influencing its cost of capital and capital structure. This section will delve into how ESG performance affects financing costs for companies across countries, exploring the underlying economic principles and market mechanisms driving this phenomenon.
Houston and Shan (2022) show that banks are more likely to grant loans to borrowers with ESG profiles similar to their own and positively influence the borrower’s subsequent ESG performance.  Conditional on obtaining new loan financing within a 2-year period centered on the end date of the original loan, they find that borrowers are less likely to renew loans with the same lead lender(s) if there was a negative ESG-related reputational shock, increasing financial costs.  Apergis et al. (2022) examined bond issuance and used corporate bond yield spreads as a proxy for financing costs, reaching comparable conclusions. Eliwa et al. (2021) provided a more detailed exploration of the theoretical mechanisms driving this impact. According to stakeholder theory, stewardship theory, and transformational leadership theory, strong ESG practices signal to creditors a company’s management efficiency and integrity, demonstrating its ability to allocate resources effectively to meet stakeholder concerns while maintaining profitability. This reduces the likelihood of decisions that prioritize shareholders at the expense of creditors, mitigating agency conflict risks. Additionally, robust ESG performance lowers the reputational risks creditors face, leading to reduced interest rate demands. Furthermore, ESG disclosure itself reduces information asymmetry between lenders and companies, thereby decreasing risks associated with investment uncertainty. 
Research focusing on Asian countries yields similar conclusions. Gracia and Siregar (2021) and Nguyen and Duong (2025) examined listed companies in ASEAN countries and Vietnam, respectively, using the ratio of a firm’s interest expense to its average net debt as a proxy for the cost of debt. Their findings still confirmed a negative relationship between ESG performance and debt financing costs. However, studies on China suggest that ESG performance significantly impacts cost of debt only when loan amounts are substantial. 
In addition to issuing bonds in public markets or securing bank loans, companies can raise funds by selling equity. This can be done by attracting venture capital or private equity investments, or, for companies eligible to go public, through an initial public offering (IPO). And companies can further raise funds through secondary stock offerings. Consequently, the cost of equity financing is a critical factor for companies to consider. Academic research estimates the cost of equity financing (i.e., investors’ expected returns) using two main approaches: the Implied Cost of Capital (ICC) and factor models based on realized returns (e.g., CAPM). The ICC is an ex-ante measure of equity cost, calculated as the internal rate of return that equates the current stock price with the present value of expected future cash flows. In contrast, factor models like CAPM are ex-post methods, relying on historical return data from market indices, government bonds, and individual stocks to estimate the cost of equity. Similar to the mechanisms by which ESG impacts the cost of debt financing, a company’s sustainability practices can theoretically create long-term value for shareholders by reducing information asymmetry, mitigating various risks in business operations, and aligning with customers’ sustainable purchasing preferences, thereby lowering the cost of equity financing. In fact, a substantial body of empirical research has tested these influence mechanisms and their effects.
A company’s sustainability performance not only affects its cost of capital but also influences its choice of financing methods, thereby shaping its capital structure. According to the pecking order theory(Myers and Majluf, 1984), differences in information asymmetry between different kinds of capital providers and the company, as well as varying sensitivities to the same information, lead to disparities in financing costs. As a result, companies follow a specific hierarchy when selecting financing methods to minimize costs. Specifically, companies prioritize using retained earnings. If internal funds are insufficient, they prefer debt financing, as creditors are less sensitive to the company’s information(Höck et al., 2023). This lower sensitivity means creditors focus primarily on the company’s ability to repay, without interpreting borrowing as a sign of operational issues that would prompt higher interest rates. Moreover, debt financing offers tax shield benefits. In contrast, equity financing (issuing new shares) is often perceived by external investors as a signal that the company’s value is overinflated or that it may be facing operational challenges, potentially leading to a stock price decline and higher financing costs. In other words, companies seek more internal financing sources (Denis and Mihov, 2003).
It’s particularly noteworthy that, Gibbons (2024) examined changes in the capital structure of listed companies across 40 countries before and after the implementation of mandatory environmental and social (E&S) disclosure requirements. The results showed a significant 0.39% increase in net equity issuance, equivalent to a 2.29% rise relative to the sample average, while net debt issuance significantly decreased. This suggests that companies tend to favor equity financing after disclosing E&S information. The study attributes this shift to a unique clientele mechanism, where an increase in institutional investors with specific E&S preferences alters the shareholder base. These investors influence corporate decision-making through engagement with management or by exercising voting rights. Through empirical testing, the authors confirmed that the rise in institutional investors who are signatories to the Principles for Responsible Investment (PRI) significantly impacts equity issuance, validating the existence of the clientele mechanism.
We have gained insights from extensive empirical research in academia about the impact of corporate sustainability practices (ESG disclosure or ESG practice) on the cost of capital and the underlying theoretical mechanisms. However, we believe what will spark greater interest is real-world examples from industry. For instance, if companies with better sustainability performance have a lower cost of capital, what is the specific difference in cost of capital between high-sustainability and low-sustainability companies? Does this difference vary in magnitude across regions or industries? How do banks in different countries incorporate a company’s sustainability into their lending decisions? How do leading asset management firms in sustainable investing identify companies resilient to sustainability-related risks? In this section, we will present a series of real-world cases to help everyone better understand the critical role of ESG in determining a company’s cost of capital.
A MSCI study allows us to clearly identify the differences in cost of capital among companies with varying ESG risk exposures. The study analyzed stocks from the MSCI ACWI Index and bonds from the MSCI Corporate Bond Index, covering 4,319 unique issuers. These issuers span developed and emerging markets across 11 industry sectors, with data from August 2015 to May 2024. The study measured cost of equity and cost of debt using two methods each. For cost of equity, the first method calculated expected stock returns using the Capital Asset Pricing Model, while the second used β (systematic risk exposure) as a proxy. For cost of debt, in addition to interest expense on debt, the study used bond issuance spreads relative to treasury yields as a proxy, as mentioned earlier. Both β and spreads are relative metrics, controlling for macro factors like differences in risk-free rates or equity-market risk premiums across regions. The cost of capital was calculated using the Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WACC). The issuers were divided into five groups based on their MSCI ESG ratings: top ESG rating (highest-scoring companies), high, mid, low (average-scoring companies), and bottom ESG rating (lowest-scoring companies), to compare the differences in their cost of capital.
We can gain a more intuitive understanding from a more micro perspective, such as the policies and practices of banks in different countries regarding sustainable lending. From the earlier chart, we can see that Chinese companies exhibit some of the largest gaps in the APAC region between high and low ESG-rated firms, whether in terms of cost of capital, cost of debt, or cost of equity. To support the national climate strategy of achieving carbon peak by 2030 and carbon neutrality by 2060, the former China Banking and Insurance Regulatory Commission issued the Guidelines for Green Finance in the Banking and Insurance Industries in 2022. These guidelines require banks to establish ESG risk assessment standards for clients and use the results as key factors in determining credit ratings, loan amounts, and interest rate pricing for enterprises. 
As early as 2021, the People’s Bank of China (PBoC), China’s central bank, introduced a structural monetary policy tool: the Carbon Emission Reduction Support Tool. This tool operates as follows: financial institutions first provide loans to enterprises in key carbon emission reduction sectors at rates aligned with the Loan Market Quotation Rate (LPR). They can then apply for funding from the PBoC’s Carbon Emission Reduction Support Tool, through which the central bank provides funds equivalent to 60% of the loan principal at an interest rate of 1.75%. For context, as of May 2025, China’s five-year interbank Loan Prime Rate is 3.50%, and the interest rate for borrowing from the PBoC’s Medium-term Lending Facility (MLF) is 2.5%. The low funding cost of 1.75% strongly incentivizes banks to offer loans for corporate carbon emission reduction projects, enhancing access to finance for green-focused enterprises, which typically perform well in the environmental dimension of ESG. 
According to the framework of the Carbon Emission Reduction Support Tool, national financial institutions utilizing this tool are required to prioritize carbon emission reduction loans for enterprises in three key areas: clean energy, energy conservation and environmental protection, and carbon reduction technologies. Additionally, the People’s Bank of China mandates that financial institutions applying for the tool provide carbon emission reduction data for the relevant loans. After receiving support, institutions must disclose quarterly to the public information including the supported carbon reduction sectors, number of projects, loan amounts, weighted average interest rates, and carbon reduction data. From the ESG risk management sections of sustainability reports from numerous major banks, it is evident that they all have similar ESG risk monitoring processes and set corresponding entry thresholds for key industries to avoid projects with high energy consumption, high emissions, excess capacity, or significant social risks. Although these banks do not explicitly mention linking ESG risks to loan interest rates, the low capital cost incentives from the central bank’s Carbon Emission Reduction Support Tool encourage them in practice to set loan rates for borrowing companies who positively pursue decarbonization slightly below the Loan Prime Rate (LPR). For example, in 2022, China Merchants Bank issued carbon emission reduction loans to 181 projects with a weighted average interest rate of 3.58%, while China Everbright Bank provided 35 million yuan in financing support to a photovoltaic company at a project loan rate as low as 3.45%, compared to the year-end LPR of 3.65%. In 2023, the Industrial and Commercial Bank of China issued carbon emission reduction loan to 841 projects, with a weighted average loan interest rate of 2.69%, while the year-end LPR was 3.45%.
Yet, this strong regulatory push also raises critical questions. Under such a framework, could high-pollution industries face a financing drought, potentially triggering negative spillovers for economic stability? When the People’s Bank of China provides low-interest refinancing through its Carbon Emission Reduction Facility, might this create over-incentivization or moral hazard, where firms rely too heavily on subsidies without pursuing genuine transformation? What safeguards can mitigate such risks? Commercial banks also confront a delicate balancing act: how can they fulfill mandates to support the green transition while still preserving profitability and managing credit risks effectively? 
As a result, major European banks have positively responded to the EU’s call to seize the commercial opportunities presented by the continent’s sustainable transition. In contrast to the top-down approach adopted by Chinese banks, as mentioned earlier, which involves leveraging the central bank’s Carbon Emission Reduction Support Tool to provide loans for sustainable activities, European banks generally adopt a self-driven approach, setting targets for sustainable investment and financing volumes, as well as mid- and long-term carbon reduction goals across all scopes. They adjust their business strategies, capital allocation, and stakeholder engagement methods based on these targets. The cases of sustainable lending policies from Chinese and European banks discussed earlier essentially demonstrate that “companies actively pursuing sustainable transformation are more likely to access bank credit resources”. However, what we may be more interested in is whether achieving specific sustainability goals allows a company to secure a lower cost of capital, such as reduced bank loan interest rates. I can confirm that this is indeed the case. 
In recent years, sustainability-linked loans (SLL) have emerged as a new sustainable financing tool, gradually gaining public attention. The world’s first sustainability-linked loan was a 1 billion euro Revolving Credit Facility signed in 2017 between Philips and a consortium of 16 banks led by ING. The innovative aspect of this loan was that its interest rate was tied to Philips’ sustainability performance, evaluated through ESG ratings provided by the third-party agency Sustainalytics. If Philips’ Sustainalytics ESG rating improved (indicating better sustainability performance), the loan interest rate would decrease, lowering the cost of financing. Conversely, if the ESG rating declined, the financing cost would increase. According to ING, the interest rate discount or premium for such SLLs typically fluctuates between 5% and 10% of the interest rate margin.

Once a company secures financing, it embarks on formal business operations, channeling funds into specific investment projects. Through these projects, the company produces goods or services to meet customer demands, aiming to maximize revenue while minimizing costs and expenses to boost profits. A company’s profits—or more precisely, investors’ expectations of future profits—drive its market value growth. In essence, profitability and the corresponding rise in market value represent a company’s core objectives in the business world. However, enhancing profitability is far from a simple accounting exercise. The factors determining revenue, costs, and expenses are complex and intertwined with the diverse interests of multiple stakeholders. For instance, employees may seek more meaningful work, access to valuable training for long-term career growth, or greater cultural and background diversity within the company to spark innovation. Upstream suppliers often prefer to partner with reputable firms that demonstrate stable, long-term profitability. Meanwhile, downstream customers, increasingly influenced by environmental consciousness, may favor products made from biodegradable materials.
The success of a company’s operations thus hinges on the interplay of these stakeholders. A critical factor is the extent to which the company addresses their varied demands. In the context of our theme, sustainable practices—such as sustainability disclosures or improved ESG performance—naturally align with the interests of these stakeholders. But do these practices truly deliver the anticipated improvements in profitability and long-term value? Over the years, academic research has produced a wealth of empirical studies that offer valuable insights into this question.
Reduced myopic actions: Greater transparency in ESG disclosures narrows the information gap between corporate managers, shareholders, and other stakeholders. This discourages managerial rent-seeking or short-term opportunistic behaviors, such as aggressive earnings management, insider trading, or related-party transactions. Moreover, sustainability policies encourage management to adopt a long-term perspective, ultimately enhancing firm value over time.
Alignment with Stakeholder Interests: ESG practices can mitigate information asymmetry between corporate managers and various stakeholders, fostering stronger relationships with them. By doing so, these practices help balance and address the diverse demands of stakeholders, reducing agency costs and ultimately promoting the long-term value of the firm. From a consumer perspective, companies with strong ESG propositions tend to build customer loyalty and attract new market segments. From a partner perspective, large suppliers increasingly integrate ESG factors into their value chains, prioritizing sustainability in water use, packaging, and plastics. These suppliers prefer to collaborate with businesses that share their values, while resource-efficient firms can also reduce per-unit production costs. From an employee perspective, younger workers and millennials seek meaningful work, and companies with sustainability commitments are better positioned to attract and retain talent, often benefiting from higher productivity and efficiency. From a regulatory perspective, policymakers rely on the corporate sector to address issues like environmental pollution and workplace diversity, while, sustainable practices like a smaller environmental footprint can also reduce the likelihood of regulatory penalties. Finally, from an investor perspective, corporates’ ESG-focused projects—such as developing renewable energy plants—help avoid stranded assets (e.g. coal-fired power plants), mitigate downside risks, and attract long-term investors with ESG preferences, ultimately lowering the firm’s cost of capital.
The findings revealed that high-sustainability companies are far more likely to assign sustainability responsibilities to their board of directors and establish a dedicated board committee for sustainability. They also tend to link executive compensation to environmental and social performance metrics. These firms typically implement more comprehensive and engaged stakeholder management processes, attract a higher proportion of long-term-oriented investors, and emphasize long-term information during conference calls with sell-side analysts. Compared to their low-sustainability counterparts, high-sustainability companies are more proactive in measuring information related to key stakeholders—such as employees, customers, and suppliers—and enhancing the credibility of these metrics through auditing procedures. Moreover, they not only collect but also disclose a greater volume of nonfinancial data. In terms of profitability, high-sustainability companies outperform their low-sustainability counterparts in both stock market and accounting performance. Using the Fama-French four-factor model to measure alpha (the stock’s abnormal return), the author found that high-sustainability companies generate significantly higher alpha than low-sustainability firms. Similarly, in accounting metrics, the former exhibit notably higher Return on Equity (ROE) and Return on Assets (ROA) compared to the latter. This superior performance is particularly pronounced in business-to-consumer (B2C) companies. The author attributes these results to the stronger brand reputation and more efficient use of natural resources by high-sustainability firms, reinforcing the impact mechanisms discussed earlier.
What explains these discrepancies? Buchanan et al. (2018)’s perspective may offer valuable explanations: They argues that the impact of corporate sustainability on profitability and market value hinges on the relative dominance of two competing mechanisms. The first mechanism, which promotes profitability, has been discussed earlier: investments in sustainability strengthen relationships with stakeholders, balancing their diverse needs—such as consumers’ demand for eco-friendly products, employees’ expectations for better welfare, and investors’ requirements for governance transparency and long-term profitability. This reduces agency conflict costs and enhances corporate reputation, ultimately boosting profitability and long-term value.
The second mechanism, however, negatively affects firm value. Drawing on overinvestment theory, authors suggests that in certain contexts—such as periods of severe financial constraints—sustainability investments become highly costly, with potential returns falling far below the firm’s cost of capital, leading to a loss of value. Through a study of US listed companies, they found that before the financial crisis, the “stakeholder engagement and agency cost reduction” mechanism prevailed, and ESG’s positive effects were evident. During the financial crisis, however, companies positively invested in sustainability lost more value compared to their control group, indicating that the overinvestment mechanism dominated.
Buchanan et al. (2018)’s explanation provides clearer insight into the conditions under which corporate ESG practices enhance profitability: When a society is not short of financial resources, has a relatively high level of legal system, and the government's image is more positive, and when all stakeholders hope to meet the needs of value realization at a higher level beyond basic survival needs, the investment of enterprises in ESG will play a positive role; conversely, if various resources in the society are scarce and the primary task of all stakeholders is to ensure the most basic survival rights and lack expectations for "additional self-actualization", the investment of enterprises in ESG will have a counterproductive effect.
Beyond the lack of societal focus on sustainability issues and constrained financial resources, cultural factors also play a significant role. For example, a 1983 study found that Malaysian companies were often reluctant to demonstrate even minimal concern for social and environmental issues (that is, they were unwilling to disclose their related social responsibility activities extensively). This reluctance stems from Malaysia’s predominantly Islamic culture, where a core principle emphasizes keeping meritorious deeds discreet. In this view, good deeds are performed for the sake of Allah, not for public display or societal recognition. Publicly disclosing such actions is often perceived as “showing off,” which conflicts with religious values and may even pose reputational risks.

Investments in sustainability not only impact a company’s profitability but also reduce its operating risks, helping to prevent financial distress, delisting, or bankruptcy. In terms of earnings risk or operating risk, Arif et al. (2024) use the level of analysts' earnings forecast dispersion as a measure of future earnings risk, and find that ESG disclosures have a significant negative impact on a firm's future earnings risk. Additionally, disaggregated analysis of ESG disclosures shows that the impact of the social dimension is economically more pronounced than the environmental and governance disclosures, indicating that the disclosure of social issues carries more value than the other dimensions of ESG disclosures in minimising the dispersion of future earnings risk for a firm. This highlight that transparent information environment could serve as a tool to minimise the agency problem arising from the information asymmetry between corporate managers and shareholders concerning firms' future earnings. examined US listed companies, assessing the impact of ESG disclosures on future risk from both accounting-based and market-based perspectives. The study measured accounting-based earnings risk using the volatility of Return on Assets (ROA), defined as the standard deviation of ROA over five-year overlapping windows. For market-based risk, it used the overall volatility of stock returns (calculated as the standard deviation of monthly stock returns) and the idiosyncratic volatility of stock returns (derived as the annualized standard deviation of residuals from the Fama-French three-factor model). The findings confirmed that ESG disclosures significantly reduce a company’s operating risk.
External Oversight Perspective: ESG disclosures reduce information asymmetry between management, investors, and other stakeholders regarding business activities. This enables shareholders and stakeholders to more effectively monitor and govern corporate behavior, increasing market and societal pressure on firms with poor ESG performance. Such pressure may lead to penalties for companies engaging in high-ESG-risk projects, such as investor divestment or consumer boycotts of the firm’s products.
Internal and External Trust Mechanism: High levels of ESG disclosure enhance a company’s social capital, such as customer loyalty, and foster enforceable mutual trust among management, shareholders, and other stakeholders. This collective trust helps curb excessive risk-taking, lowers the cost of equity capital, and encourages shareholders to support low-risk projects that ensure sustainable development(Godfrey, 2005). Meanwhile, management benefits from more stable career prospects by avoiding high-risk initiatives(Hoi et al., 2019).
However, some studies have reached the opposite conclusion. Hsiao et al. (2022), using the Z-score as a measure of financial health and analyzing Chinese listed companies between 2019 and the third quarter of 2021, observed that during the pandemic, firms with stronger ESG performance often exhibited weaker overall financial conditions. This counterintuitive result can be explained through the lens of overinvestment theory. From 2019 to 2022, the global economy faced structural recessionary pressures triggered by the COVID-19 crisis. Firms encountered shrinking revenues, heightened uncertainty, and severe constraints on financial resources. In this environment, companies that continued to allocate substantial funds to ESG initiatives faced a trade-off between long-term sustainability goals and short-term survival. During an acute economic downturn, ESG spending may crowd out the resources needed to stabilize core operations. 
From an investor’s perspective, ESG data remains a valuable lens for risk assessment—but the challenge lies in interpretation. Rather than assuming that higher ESG scores are universally desirable, investors must weigh the trade-off between long-term resilience and short-term fragility. A more nuanced strategy would be to evaluate whether a firm’s ESG initiatives are strategically aligned with its financial capacity, sectoral dynamics, and macroeconomic environment. Ultimately, the key lies not only in measuring ESG outcomes, but in asking whether firms are pursuing sustainability at a pace that is financially sustainable.

In addition to the factors mentioned above, Tufano et al. (2024) argue that a company’s ownership structure significantly influences its sustainability performance. By analyzing 26,481 firm-year observations from 3,083 listed companies across 62 countries between 2002 and 2019, the study found that companies with founding families or other individual investors as material owners (i.e., where founding family members or individual investors are among the top ten shareholders) perform worse in ESG compared to firms with only institutional owners (which serve as the baseline category). This suggests that family or individual investors may prioritize short-term financial returns over long-term ESG goals. However, when family members serve as CEOs, the company’s ESG performance is significantly better than that of other firms, with an even stronger positive effect when descendants serve as CEOs (the coefficient for descendant CEOs is approximately double that of founding CEOs). This indicates that family involvement in management roles may drive longer-term, stakeholder-focused strategies, and generational succession in family firms may foster a stronger ESG orientation. Companies with non-family managers or government entities as major shareholders generally perform better in ESG, likely because government owners are driven by public policy or societal expectations to support environmental and social goals, while non-family managers may use ESG investments to reduce conflicts with stakeholders, thereby enhancing corporate reputation and operational stability. Companies with other listed firms as major shareholders tend to have poorer ESG performance, as these shareholder firms may prioritize short-term financial synergies or market competition over sustainable goals.

Under the broad umbrella of sustainable investing, asset owners and managers have developed several distinct investment strategies and reached a basic consensus on their specific meanings. Let’s start by introducing the Global Sustainable Investment Alliance (GSIA). Founded in 2010, the GSIA is responsible for aggregating responsible investment market data from its members, such as the European Sustainable Investment Forum, Japan Sustainable Investment Forum, and China Sustainable Investment Forum. Its goal is to analyze the global sustainable investment market and the evolution of ESG trends. In particular, the GSIA has detailed the different types of mainstream sustainable investment strategies in its reports. Specifically, these strategies are: Negative Screening (or Worst-in-Class) & Exclusion Policy; Norms-based screening; Positive (or best-in-class) screening; ESG Integration; Thematic investing; Active ownership (voting policy & shareholder engagement); Impact investing. Next, I will explain the specific meanings of these sustainable investment strategies one by one and use real-world examples to help everyone fully understand how these strategies are applied in actual investment environments. 
Active ownership seeks to influence corporate behavior and promote improvements in environmental, social, and governance practices through shareholder engagement and voting. This is done through direct communication with company management or boards, submitting or co-filing shareholder proposals, and proxy voting in line with ESG guidelines. For example, investors might engage with target companies on specific topics such as climate target-setting, implementation of emissions reduction strategies, employee pay ratios, or avoiding animal testing. They may also file shareholder resolutions or even participate in public litigation. If a company refuses to engage or fails to change its unsustainable practices—such as continuing to invest in coal-fired power plants—investors may divest. This strategy not only helps improve a company’s ESG performance but also creates long-term value for investors.

We can all understand that improvements in a company’s profitability (earnings growth) and dividend increases (dividend yield) fundamentally stem from sustained enhancements in its fundamentals. As discussed in Chapter 2, these enhancements include reduced financing costs, lower costs from increased employee efficiency, decreased agency costs with stakeholders, or improved profitability from capitalizing on the purchasing preferences of customers and supply chain partners. Broadly, these can be summarized as an increased likelihood of generating profits (higher cash flows) and a reduced likelihood of losses (or lower risks). According to Giese et al.
