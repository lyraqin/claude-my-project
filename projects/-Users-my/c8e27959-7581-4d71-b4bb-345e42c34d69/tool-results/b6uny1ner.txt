Reliable data is essential for evaluating sustainability strategies. The ESG ratings industry is led by firms such as ISS, MSCI, LSEG, S&P Global, Sustainalytics, and Moody’s. These organizations assess companies on ESG criteria, helping investors make informed decisions. New entrants (e.g., Covalence, OWL ESG, and Truvalue Labs) are using advanced technologies like artificial intelligence, big data, and natural language processing to improve analysis. Despite concerns about the consistency and transparency of ESG scores, ESG ratings have helped raise standards and improve transparency across industries.
Investors, asset managers, and financial products
Investors are at the heart of the ESG ecosystem, using sustainability information to guide investment and risk management. Asset owners include pension funds, sovereign wealth funds, insurance companies, endowments, family offices, and retail investors. Asset managers oversee funds for these investors, including mutual funds, hedge funds, private equity, and infrastructure funds. On the financing side, banks and issuers structure ESG products such as green bonds and assess ESG risks when issuing debt.
As ESG has become more prominent, concerns about greenwashing and inconsistent practices have grown. Regulators and supervisors now play a key role in shaping the ESG landscape. International bodies like the Basel Committee on Banking Supervision (BCBS), the International Association of Insurance Supervisors (IAIS), the International Organization of Securities Commissions (IOSCO), and the Financial Stability Board (FSB) have recognized sustainable risk alongside traditional financial risks. Governments are also active: the European Union leads with comprehensive ESG regulations, the UK has integrated ESG into corporate governance, and exchanges like the Hong Kong Stock Exchange require ESG reporting for listed companies.

A company’s financing activities form the foundational step for undertaking various projects. The core objective of financing is to secure the necessary funds to achieve strategic and operational goals. Broadly, financing can be divided into two categories: equity financing and debt financing. Equity financing involves raising funds by selling company shares, allowing investors to become shareholders and share in the company’s profits and growth potential, though it dilutes existing shareholders’ ownership. For instance, in 2022, Chinese wind power company Sany Renewable Energy conducted an initial public offering, raising nearly 5.5 billion RMB to fund production line upgrades, turbine R&D, and wind power industrial park projects. Debt financing, on the other hand, involves raising funds through issuing bonds or borrowing from financial institutions, requiring the company to repay the principal and interest within an agreed timeframe. For example, in 2022, Danish turbine manufacturer Vestas secured a 475 million EUR green loan from the European Investment Bank to support R&D for large-megawatt turbines. The choice between these financing methods depends not only on a company’s financial health, market conditions, and industry characteristics but also on external factors such as macroeconomic policies, financial market volatility, and investor preferences. In recent years, as concepts like sustainability and climate change have gained prominence, regulators and financial systems worldwide have increasingly integrated companies’ ESG performance into regulatory and investment decisions. Consequently, a company’s sustainability performance has become a key factor influencing its cost of capital and capital structure. This section will delve into how ESG performance affects financing costs for companies across countries, exploring the underlying economic principles and market mechanisms driving this phenomenon.
Sustainability and cost of debt
Developed economies, particularly the European Union, have taken the lead in integrating sustainability into financial systems. The EU’s 2018 Sustainable Finance Action Plan highlighted that achieving the 2030 energy and climate goals—reducing greenhouse gas emissions by 40% compared to 1990 levels, ensuring at least 27% of final energy consumption from renewables, and achieving at least 30% energy savings—requires an annual investment of €180 billion. Extending to sectors like transport, water, and waste would necessitate €270 billion. To align such large-scale funding with EU sustainability objectives, the EU established the EU Taxonomy, a unified classification system for climate, environmental, and socially sustainable activities, defining what qualifies as sustainable and identifying investment areas with the greatest sustainability impac. Additionally, the EU’s Sustainable Finance Disclosure Regulation (SFDR) mandates disclosures at both product and entity levels. At the product level, portfolios managed by banks and investment firms, UCITS funds (Undertakings for Collective Investment in Transferable Securities), AIFs (Alternative Investment Funds), insurance-based investment products, and pension products must disclose their sustainability impacts (e.g., the proportion of the portfolio aligned with the EU Taxonomy) and the impact of sustainability risks on product returns. At the entity level, financial firms must disclose their sustainability risk policies and the Principal Adverse Impacts of their lending or investment decisions (e.g., GHG intensity of investee companies or exposure to controversial weapons). Clearly, these EU regulations have made it imperative for financial institutions to incorporate sustainability factors into lending or investment decisions (in this section, specifically bond investments).
To investigate whether corporate sustainability practices genuinely impact financing costs and to understand the mechanisms behind such effects, academic research has produced substantial empirical evidence from both developed and developing economies. Using a sample of global companies from 2002 to 2021
Houston and Shan (2022) show that banks are more likely to grant loans to borrowers with ESG profiles similar to their own and positively influence the borrower’s subsequent ESG performance.  Conditional on obtaining new loan financing within a 2-year period centered on the end date of the original loan, they find that borrowers are less likely to renew loans with the same lead lender(s) if there was a negative ESG-related reputational shock, increasing financial costs.  Apergis et al. (2022) examined bond issuance and used corporate bond yield spreads as a proxy for financing costs, reaching comparable conclusions. Eliwa et al. (2021) provided a more detailed exploration of the theoretical mechanisms driving this impact. According to stakeholder theory, stewardship theory, and transformational leadership theory, strong ESG practices signal to creditors a company’s management efficiency and integrity, demonstrating its ability to allocate resources effectively to meet stakeholder concerns while maintaining profitability. This reduces the likelihood of decisions that prioritize shareholders at the expense of creditors, mitigating agency conflict risks. Additionally, robust ESG performance lowers the reputational risks creditors face, leading to reduced interest rate demands. Furthermore, ESG disclosure itself reduces information asymmetry between lenders and companies, thereby decreasing risks associated with investment uncertainty.

Sustainability and cost of equity
In addition to issuing bonds in public markets or securing bank loans, companies can raise funds by selling equity. This can be done by attracting venture capital or private equity investments, or, for companies eligible to go public, through an initial public offering (IPO). And companies can further raise funds through secondary stock offerings. Consequently, the cost of equity financing is a critical factor for companies to consider. Academic research estimates the cost of equity financing (i.e., investors’ expected returns) using two main approaches: the Implied Cost of Capital (ICC) and factor models based on realized returns (e.g., CAPM). The ICC is an ex-ante measure of equity cost, calculated as the internal rate of return that equates the current stock price with the present value of expected future cash flows. In contrast, factor models like CAPM are ex-post methods, relying on historical return data from market indices, government bonds, and individual stocks to estimate the cost of equity. Similar to the mechanisms by which ESG impacts the cost of debt financing, a company’s sustainability practices can theoretically create long-term value for shareholders by reducing information asymmetry, mitigating various risks in business operations, and aligning with customers’ sustainable purchasing preferences, thereby lowering the cost of equity financing. In fact, a substantial body of empirical research has tested these influence mechanisms and their effects.
Li et al. (2024) used a sample of Chinese listed companies from 2009 to 2020 to empirically analyze changes in the cost of equity after firms received ESG ratings from SynTao, a Chinese rating agency. They measured the cost of equity using the Implied Cost of Capital method. The results showed that, after receiving a SynTao ESG rating, a company’s cost of equity decreased by 45.6 basis points compared to before. The authors attributed this reduction to two factors: first, a decrease in information asymmetry between investors and the company, which reduces investment uncertainty; second, the soft market constraints imposed by third-party ESG ratings, which establishes an effective external monitoring mechanism and then increases the cost of non-compliance, encouraging prudent business operations, reducing corporate operational risks, and consequently lowering investors’ risk premiums. Both mechanisms have been repeatedly discussed earlier. The study empirically validated the presence of these mechanisms. The authors used the number of securities analysts covering a company and the number of research reports about this company as proxies for information asymmetry, finding that both significantly increased after a company received an ESG rating, with the effect being more pronounced for firms with previously high information asymmetry. Besides, the authors measured operational risk using the standard deviation of return on assets volatility over a continuous three-year period and the annual standard deviation of daily individual stock returns, respectively, confirming that receiving an ESG rating can significantly reduce a company’s operational risk. Ng and Rezaee (2015) and Lynch and O'Hagan-Luff (2024), in their empirical studies of global listed companies, similarly concluded that improved corporate sustainability performance reduces the cost of equity financing, and only environmental and governance sustainability performance dimensions contribute to this relationship，while social sustainability performance is only marginally, related to cost of equity. And the relationship between ESG and cost of euquity is strengthened when  economic sustainability disclosure is strong.
with this effect being more pronounced in countries with stringent sustainability-focused institutional environments. Building on this, Lynch and O'Hagan-Luff (2024) further found that the suppressive effect of enhanced ESG ratings on the cost of equity exhibits a diminishing marginal trend.
Merton (1987) capital market equilibrium model posits that expanding a firm’s investor base leads to a lower cost of capital and higher market value. Therefore, it is necessary to examine whether a company’s sustainability performance influences institutional investor ownership, as this indirectly affects the cost of capital. Gibbons (2024) investigated changes in institutional investor ownership of global listed companies before and after the implementation of mandatory E&S disclosure regulations across more than 40 countries. Initially, the authors were skeptical about whether investors genuinely paid attention to corporate E&S information, as many institutional investors claim to invest only in companies with ESG ratings, yet most asset managers lack explicit mandates or policies to prioritize environmental and social goals. They might merely engage in “cheap talk” to enhance their reputation without truly valuing corporate sustainability information. However, empirical results showed that institutional investors do care about this information, with institutional ownership increasing by 0.66% following the implementation of disclosure regulations. This represents a 1.3% increase relative to the sample average. Moreover, the authors found that the additional investors were long-term-oriented institutional investors with ESG preferences, specifically those who are signatories to the Principles for Responsible Investment (PRI).

MSCI evidence: ESG and cost of capital
We have gained insights from extensive empirical research in academia about the impact of corporate sustainability practices (ESG disclosure or ESG practice) on the cost of capital and the underlying theoretical mechanisms. However, we believe what will spark greater interest is real-world examples from industry. For instance, if companies with better sustainability performance have a lower cost of capital, what is the specific difference in cost of capital between high-sustainability and low-sustainability companies? Does this difference vary in magnitude across regions or industries? How do banks in different countries incorporate a company’s sustainability into their lending decisions? How do leading asset management firms in sustainable investing identify companies resilient to sustainability-related risks? In this section, we will present a series of real-world cases to help everyone better understand the critical role of ESG in determining a company’s cost of capital.
A MSCI study allows us to clearly identify the differences in cost of capital among companies with varying ESG risk exposures. The study analyzed stocks from the MSCI ACWI Index and bonds from the MSCI Corporate Bond Index, covering 4,319 unique issuers. These issuers span developed and emerging markets across 11 industry sectors, with data from August 2015 to May 2024. The study measured cost of equity and cost of debt using two methods each. For cost of equity, the first method calculated expected stock returns using the Capital Asset Pricing Model, while the second used β (systematic risk exposure) as a proxy. For cost of debt, in addition to interest expense on debt, the study used bond issuance spreads relative to treasury yields as a proxy, as mentioned earlier. Both β and spreads are relative metrics, controlling for macro factors like differences in risk-free rates or equity-market risk premiums across regions. The cost of capital was calculated using the Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WACC). The issuers were divided into five groups based on their MSCI ESG ratings: top ESG rating (highest-scoring companies), high, mid, low (average-scoring companies), and bottom ESG rating (lowest-scoring companies), to compare the differences in their cost of capital.

Case: Policy-driven green credit from China’s banks
We can gain a more intuitive understanding from a more micro perspective, such as the policies and practices of banks in different countries regarding sustainable lending. From the earlier chart, we can see that Chinese companies exhibit some of the largest gaps in the APAC region between high and low ESG-rated firms, whether in terms of cost of capital, cost of debt, or cost of equity. To support the national climate strategy of achieving carbon peak by 2030 and carbon neutrality by 2060, the former China Banking and Insurance Regulatory Commission issued the Guidelines for Green Finance in the Banking and Insurance Industries in 2022. These guidelines require banks to establish ESG risk assessment standards for clients and use the results as key factors in determining credit ratings, loan amounts, and interest rate pricing for enterprises. 
As early as 2021, the People’s Bank of China (PBoC), China’s central bank, introduced a structural monetary policy tool: the Carbon Emission Reduction Support Tool. This tool operates as follows: financial institutions first provide loans to enterprises in key carbon emission reduction sectors at rates aligned with the Loan Market Quotation Rate (LPR). They can then apply for funding from the PBoC’s Carbon Emission Reduction Support Tool, through which the central bank provides funds equivalent to 60% of the loan principal at an interest rate of 1.75%. For context, as of May 2025, China’s five-year interbank Loan Prime Rate is 3.50%, and the interest rate for borrowing from the PBoC’s Medium-term Lending Facility (MLF) is 2.5%. The low funding cost of 1.75% strongly incentivizes banks to offer loans for corporate carbon emission reduction projects, enhancing access to finance for green-focused enterprises, which typically perform well in the environmental dimension of ESG. 
According to the framework of the Carbon Emission Reduction Support Tool, national financial institutions utilizing this tool are required to prioritize carbon emission reduction loans for enterprises in three key areas: clean energy, energy conservation and environmental protection, and carbon reduction technologies. Additionally, the People’s Bank of China mandates that financial institutions applying for the tool provide carbon emission reduction data for the relevant loans. After receiving support, institutions must disclose quarterly to the public information including the supported carbon reduction sectors, number of projects, loan amounts, weighted average interest rates, and carbon reduction data. Case: The rise of sustainability-linked loans (SLLs)
The cases of sustainable lending policies from Chinese and European banks discussed earlier essentially demonstrate that “companies actively pursuing sustainable transformation are more likely to access bank credit resources”. However, what we may be more interested in is whether achieving specific sustainability goals allows a company to secure a lower cost of capital, such as reduced bank loan interest rates. I can confirm that this is indeed the case. 
In recent years, sustainability-linked loans (SLL) have emerged as a new sustainable financing tool, gradually gaining public attention. The world’s first sustainability-linked loan was a 1 billion euro Revolving Credit Facility signed in 2017 between Philips and a consortium of 16 banks led by ING. The innovative aspect of this loan was that its interest rate was tied to Philips’ sustainability performance, evaluated through ESG ratings provided by the third-party agency Sustainalytics. If Philips’ Sustainalytics ESG rating improved (indicating better sustainability performance), the loan interest rate would decrease, lowering the cost of financing. Conversely, if the ESG rating declined, the financing cost would increase. According to ING, the interest rate discount or premium for such SLLs typically fluctuates between 5% and 10% of the interest rate margin. 
The introduction of this loan spurred rapid growth in the sustainability-linked loan market and led to the release of the Sustainability Linked Loan Principles (SLLP) in March 2019 by the Loan Market Association (LMA), the Loan Syndications and Trading Association (LSTA), and the Asia Pacific Loan Market Association (APLMA), providing a standardized framework for subsequent similar loans.
According to statistics from Japan’s Ministry of the Environment, the global sustainability-linked loan (SLL) market was only 6.5 billion USD in 2017 but grew to 673.7 billion USD by 2022. Although it declined to 489.3 million USD in 2024, the absolute value remains significant. In terms of regional distribution, Europe has consistently led the world in SLLs, followed by North America, while the Asia-Pacific (APAC) region has also gained considerable scale since 2021.

Current consumer demand trends further prompt us to ask: Why do Asia-Pacific consumers tend to prioritize health over low price, while European and American consumers often place stronger emphasis on cost-efficiency? This divergence suggests that firms must adopt differentiated product and marketing strategies tailored to local sustainability preferences. For example, in the Asia-Pacific market, companies may find greater success by highlighting nutritional value, wellness benefits, and product safety, whereas in Western markets, strategies emphasizing affordability and accessibility of sustainable products may resonate more strongly.
Another critical challenge lies in narrowing the gap between consumers’ stated willingness to pay a premium for sustainable products and their actual purchasing behavior. Firms can address this discrepancy by improving transparency, strengthening trust through credible certifications, and communicating the long-term value of sustainability in terms that are tangible to everyday life. Digital tools, such as personalized recommendations or gamified loyalty programs, can also help translate sustainable intentions into consistent purchasing behavior.
Finally, the rise of sustainable consumption trends could intensify competition between multinational corporations and local enterprises. Multinationals often possess the resources to scale sustainable innovation rapidly, but local firms may enjoy an advantage in understanding cultural preferences and consumer psychology. This dynamic may accelerate both product innovation and market segmentation, reshaping the global competitive landscape in favor of firms that can most effectively integrate sustainability with local consumer expectations.
In summary, sustainable consumption trends have become mainstream particularly in the Asia-Pacific region, consumer goods companies should seize this opportunity to integrate sustainability into their value chains, producing safe, healthy, and green products. Companies that align with sustainable consumption demands will have the opportunity to significantly increase their income, enhance profitability, and boost long-term value.
Case: NaiSnow – green and healthy 
In recent years, growing consumer awareness of sustainability has fundamentally reshaped the strategic priorities of global brands. As environmental consciousness and health considerations increasingly influence purchasing behavior, many companies are rethinking how they design and market their products. NaiSnow (奈雪的茶), one of China’s leading tea beverage chains, has astutely identified the dual demand of Generation Z for healthier options and eco-friendly practices. This awareness has guided the firm to innovate both its product offerings and its environmental initiatives, positioning itself as a pioneer of “green and healthy” consumption in the competitive new tea beverage market.
From the outset, NaiSnow distinguished itself by prioritizing product quality and health benefits. Instead of relying on tea powder, creamers, or sugary syrups—ingredients commonly found in the industry—the company uses premium loose-leaf tea, fresh milk, and real fruits to craft its drinks. Its low-sugar recipes aim to offer consumers a more balanced and diverse nutritional experience. Over time, the firm has expanded this approach beyond beverages, launching nutritious meal-replacement shakes and bottled sparkling water to meet growing demand for health-oriented retail products. In 2022, NaiSnow replaced artificial sweeteners across all its stores with monk fruit extract, a natural sugar substitute. This move not only aligned with consumers’ health preferences but also addressed their concerns regarding the safety and nutritional value of synthetic sweeteners.

NaiSnow’s strategic initiatives illustrate how a new generation of tea beverage companies is rapidly adapting to shifting consumer preferences. By actively responding to the growing demand for healthier and more sustainable consumption, the company has not only opened new revenue streams but has also contributed to upgrading products and services across the industry, thereby enhancing consumer well-being. However, from an environmental perspective, NaiSnow’s practices remain insufficient. This asymmetric sustainability strategy—emphasizing health while neglecting environmental responsibility—invites broader reflection on whether differences in environmental information disclosure within the same industry could affect firms’ long-term competitiveness and capital market reputation. In the context of increasingly stringent regulations and the rapid development of green finance, companies that fail to provide adequate environmental disclosure may face heightened policy and reputational risks.
More fundamentally, this raises a critical question: can health- and sustainability-oriented strategies truly compensate for financial vulnerabilities in a highly competitive market? The tea beverage sector has relatively low entry barriers, intense brand competition, and strict demands for cost control and operational efficiency. If a firm’s “sustainability transition” remains limited to product innovation or branding narratives, without building structural advantages in areas such as supply chain management and operations, such strategies may struggle to support long-term profitability.
From the investor’s perspective, whether these strategic shifts are fully “priced in” is also an important and practical issue. On one hand, as sustainable development gains traction in capital markets, investors are paying increasing attention to corporate social responsibility and long-term value creation. Companies that emphasize health and sustainability may benefit from stronger brand premiums, more stable market shares, and easier access to financing. On the other hand, if their sustainability strategies lack substance or financial backing, investors may view them merely as short-term marketing tools rather than genuine sources of long-term competitiveness.
Ultimately, NaiSnow’s case reflects a broader trend: at the intersection of consumption upgrading and the sustainable movement, the ability of firms to strike a balance between health, environmental responsibility, and financial resilience is becoming a decisive factor shaping their future value.
Case: Arc'teryx ESG reputation crisis
Arc'teryx, a Canadian high-end outdoor sportswear brand, has long positioned itself as a champion of sustainability. The company proudly declares its commitment to “reducing its impact on the planet”, a message that resonates strongly with affluent, environmentally conscious outdoor enthusiasts. However, this carefully cultivated image was abruptly undermined on September 19, 2025, when Arc'teryx staged a fireworks art performance in the Himalayas—an event that drew intense public criticism for its ecological impact. The stark contrast between the company’s stated environmental values and its actual actions triggered a significant trust crisis, raising uncomfortable questions about corporate authenticity in the era of ESG branding.
The irony of this incident is amplified by Arc'teryx’s otherwise exemplary sustainability strategy. Over the years, the company has embedded sustainability into nearly every stage of its operations—from product design and material sourcing to supply chain management and brand storytelling. Unlike many fast-fashion competitors, Arc'teryx deliberately avoids frequent product launches. Instead, it emphasizes durability and technical excellence, using high-performance materials such as GORE-TEX waterproof layers and precision multi-stitching. Many of its products can last for over a decade, aligning with a growing consumer trend among outdoor enthusiasts who prefer long-term investment in premium, hard-wearing gear over disposable consumption patterns.
