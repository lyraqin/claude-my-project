    10→Climate Policy Uncertainty and Corporate Real Investment: Evidence from China
    11→
    12→Abstract
    13→In response to escalating climate change, governments worldwide have launched climate policies, often with uncertain timing, intensity, and content. This paper investigates the effect of climate policy uncertainty (CPU) on corporate real investment, using city-level CPU indices and panel data on Chinese A-share listed firms (2007–2023). Results show that elevated CPU significantly curtails real investment, a result that is robust to multiple tests. Mechanism analysis indicates that CPU restricts investment by tightening financial constraints and weakening cash flows. The negative effects of CPU are stronger for firms in underdeveloped regions, private firms, firms in less competitive markets, manufacturing firms and those in labor intensive industries, while ESG-active firms exhibit greater resilience. Within supply chains, firms can mitigate the adverse impact of CPU by securing more upstream trade credit, limiting downstream liquidity transfers, reducing dependence on major customers and enhancing cooperations with suppliers. These findings highlight the importance of stable and transparent climate policies for sustainable investment and long-term economic development.
    14→Keywords: Climate policy uncertainty, Corporate investment, Financing constraints, Cash flow

    18→	•	Introduction
    19→In recent decades, climate change, including frequent extreme events like heatwaves, droughts, and floods, is far beyond the scope of environmental problems. It has become a profound systemic risk, penetrating social and economic structures and undermining their stability. The consequences are multifaceted: public health deteriorates as extreme weather events intensify, social inequalities deepen as vulnerable communities face disproportionate exposure, and productivity across key sectors declines, ultimately constraining economic growth (Carleton and Hsiang, 2016).
    20→To address these challenges, since the Paris agreement, over 140 countries have pledged carbon neutrality and actively engage in climate governance. As the world’s largest carbon emitter and energy consumer, China has recently elevated climate governance to a national strategic priority, committing to peak carbon emissions by 2030 and achieve carbon neutrality by 2060. Sound and well-designed policies play a pivotal role in driving the green and low-carbon transformation of the social sectors, thereby supporting more sustainable patterns of economic growth. Nevertheless, the timing, intensity, and specifics of climate policies remain difficult for micro-level actors to fully anticipate, generating significant uncertainty. These policy-related uncertainties can alter energy consumption behavior (Chiu and Lee, 2020) and undermine the effectiveness of emission-reduction efforts, and even impede technological innovation and industrial transformation (Fuss et al., 2009).
    21→Existing studies have largely focused on how physical climate risks shape firms’ operational activities. Empirical evidence indicates that extreme temperature conditions substantially diminish corporate revenues (Pankratz et al., 2023), flood events adversely affect total factor productivity (Hsiang, 2010), and drought conditions have been shown to raise firms’ costs of accessing equity financing (Huynh et al., 2020). However, there are also considerable influence of climate policy interventions on business performance. For example, evidence suggests that the implementation of energy taxes can substantially affect industry-level total factor productivity (Gonseth et al., 2015), and carbon pricing mechanisms serve as effective incentives for corporate carbon mitigation strategies (Kim and Bae, 2022). Focusing on inherent policy uncertainty, Ren et al. (2022) use CPU index developed by Gavriilidis (2021) and identify firms in the mining, manufacturing, and energy production and supply industries as their research sample, showing that the inherent uncertainty associated with climate policy frameworks constrains free cash flow, and impairs R&D investment, thereby further reducing total productivity. However, the study has a relatively limited sample scope and focuses on R&D investment as the transmission mechanism, without examining how climate policy uncertainty affects firms’ overall investment opportunities and the mechanisms driving these effects. Moreover, Zhang et al. (2025) similarly use the CPU index introduced by Gavriilidis (2021) and further identify the negative effect of climate policy uncertainty on corporate investment efficiency by harming corporate governance, without necessarily diminishing free cash flow. While these studies addressed the negative impacts of climate policy risks on firms’ investment performance and potential mechanisms, they choose the CPU index introduced by Gavriilidis (2021), which measures the level of CPU in the US, to represent the general global climate risks. This method neglects the difference between US and China policy change, such as China’s consistent approve to Paris agreement, and US’multiple retreat. It is more plausible to explore Chinese identical climate policy change and its effects on corporate business decisions.
    22→Notably, Huang and Sun (2024) use keywords searching method to construct a China's national climate policy uncertainty index and shows that higher climate policy uncertainty can lower the level of firms’ investment, highlighting potential channel of investment irreversibility. However, the national index neglects the regional climate conditions and policy difference, and constraint further control or explore the effects of regional characteristics. Government policy and spending significantly affects firms’ capital investment, especially under uncertainty circumstance (Gulen and Ion, 2016). Besides, prior extensive studies suggest that heightened macroeconomic policy uncertainty tends to induce firms to delay investment decisions by multiple channels, including investment irreversibility (Gulen and Ion, 2016), financing constraint (Wen et al., 2022), increasing cash holdings (Li et al., 2023). It is still essential to consider the regional government policy difference, use more concrete data to explore the effect of Chinese local policy uncertainty, and examine more potential mechanisms. 
    23→What’s more, supply chains relationships and firms’ bargaining power also impacts firms’ investment preference by limiting their cash flow (Yu et al., 2024). Firms’ climate risks exposure can worsen their supply chain relationships and operational performance, and this effect is related to national adaptation policies and abilities (Pankratz and Schiller, 2024). Few studies have investigated the role of supply chains relationships on firms’ investment decisions under climate policy risks.
    24→Given the escalating of climate change and policies, investigating the impact of policy risks on firm’s investment behavior, depending on the understanding of potential channels and the role of supply chains relationships, is essential for understanding firms’ overall climate risk exposure and decision-making process. This can further provide important insights for designing effective climate policy tools, catalyzing the low carbon transformation of firms, maintaining economic vigor, and promoting sustainable green growth.
    25→This study examines the impact and underlying mechanisms of climate policy uncertainty on corporate real investment. Considering that China formally released its accounting standards system aligned with International Financial Reporting Standards in 2006 and began implementing it in 2007, this paper focuses on Chinese A-share listed companies over the 2007–2023 period, with 2007 marking the beginning of the sample. Our analysis indicates that increases in climate policy uncertainty substantially constrain firms’ real investment activities, primarily by tightening financing conditions and lowering available cash flows. This adverse effect is especially severe for companies situated in economically lagging regions, operating in less competitive sectors, privately owned, or exhibiting weaker ESG performance. Additionally, firms in the manufacturing sector—particularly those in labor-intensive industries—face greater exposure to climate risks and demonstrate the strongest sensitivity to policy-related uncertainty. Additionally, CPU may affect firms’ real investment by impacting trade credits resources. We find that firms may alleviate the negative consequences of climate policy uncertainty by securing additional trade credit from upstream suppliers and limiting liquidity outflows to downstream clients, while lower customer and higher supplier concentration is beneficial to maintain trade credit advantage.
    26→To mitigate endogeneity concerns, we exploit the exogenous policy shock introduced by Paris Agreement, which is considered as the most influential and wide-ranging climate policy globally in the past decade, and effectively altered the micro participants’ expectations to regarding to future climate regulatory risks (Cao et al., 2025). To assess the impact of the agreement on corporate investment decisions, we utilize a difference-in-differences (DID) framework to compare investment behavior prior to and following its implementation. To ensure the robustness of our findings, we further perform dynamic effect analyses and implement propensity score matching (PSM) techniques. Consistently, the evidence shows that the policy shock significantly curtails firms’ real investment.
    27→This paper makes several important contributions. First, it expands the scope of climate change research from physical climate risks to local climate policy uncertainty considering regional difference by using more concrete city-level index developed by Ma et al. (2023). This approach promotes the precision of quantifying the impact of macro-level climate policies and provides both theoretical insights and practical implications for regional environmental governance and corporate decision-making. 
    28→Second, considering the past limited research on the influencing mechanisms of climate policy uncertainty, especially concerns about cash flow (Li et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2025), this research uncovers that climate policy uncertainty hampers real investment primarily via two mechanisms: increased financing restrictions and lower operational cash flows. This approach deepens insight into the ways climate transition risks shape firm behavior, as well as the interactions among climate-related risks, broader macroeconomic uncertainty, and corporate decision-making processes.
    29→Finally, the paper explores multiple cross-section analysis, especially about the role of ESG practice and supply chains. We show that firms with stronger ESG practices are more resilient to such uncertainty, complementing the research about ESG performance and climate risks. The study also reveal that firms’ trade credit can be another financing sources under climate risks, and concentration levels within supply chains can be critical determinants for accessing stable trade credits. These findings complement research on supply chain relationships, climate risks, and corporate investment, further provide valuable implications for corporate supply chain risk management.
    30→The study proceeds as follows. Section 2 reviews the relevant literature and formulates the research hypotheses. Section 3 describes the data sources and empirical methodology. Section 4 presents the baseline regression results. Section 5 further investigates a series of robustness checks including the policy shock of the Paris Agreement. Section 6 presents the analysis of underlying mechanisms, followed by the cross-sectional analysis in Section 7. Section 8 concludes the study.
    31→	•	Literature review and hypothesis development
    32→Climate change affects not only fundamental economic activities such as agricultural production and resource allocation (Kates et al., 1985) but also deeply influences societal organization, cultural characteristics, and political structures (Schneider et al., 2013). Its impacts permeate multiple levels of society and individual life, exhibiting broad, profound, and systemic features that shape government policymaking. Deryugina (2017) demonstrates that post-disaster government transfer payments increase substantially through a longitudinal analysis of hurricane events, with their present value far exceeding that of direct disaster aid. This sustained fiscal loosening poses risks to public finance sustainability, alters interregional resource distribution, and ultimately affects economic development in a significant manner. Moreover, Wang et al. (2024) find that robust climate policy frameworks contribute to lower macro-financial risks, as evidenced by notable decreases in debt and currency exposure in developed countries enforcing stringent climate regulations. The formulation and execution of climate policy is a complex, multidimensional process that demands a dynamic balance between economic growth and emission reduction goals, government intervention and market mechanisms, as well as national interests and regional disparities.
    33→In recent years, the frequency and intensity of extreme climate events have continued to rise, compounded by energy security crises triggered by escalating geopolitical conflicts and the accelerated advancement of global climate governance. Together, these factors create a highly uncertain policy environment that profoundly affects corporate development strategies (Ren et al., 2022) and energy consumption patterns (Gao et al., 2024). Through textual analysis, Gavriilidis (2021) develops an index capturing climate policy uncertainty, enabling a systematic assessment of its broad economic effects. Bouri et al. (2022) show that climate policy uncertainty significantly influences the comparative returns of green and brown energy equities, offering new perspectives for investment risk management and allocation strategies. Meanwhile, Gao et al. (2024) find that such uncertainty amplifies extreme risk transmissions between the broader economy and metal prices, with consequential effects on commodity market stability. For financial institutions, climate policy uncertainty significantly alters banks’ risk preferences, reducing both active and passive risk-taking and increasing bankruptcy risk (Dai and Zhang, 2023). In the industrial economy, empirical evidence from (Lin, 2024) indicates that climate policy uncertainty substantially reduces urban energy efficiency, constraining industrial upgrading and energy transition. At the firm level, Ren et al. (2022) demonstrate that climate policy uncertainty lowers R&D investment and cash flow, significantly suppressing total factor productivity. Collectively, these studies underscore the potential threats of climate policy uncertainty to macroeconomic stability and financial markets, yet research focusing on its impact on corporate performance remains limited.
    34→On the other hand, while a growing body of research has examined how climate-related factors influence corporate investment decisions, research perspectives remain limited, mainly covering physical climate risks and climate policies. Xu et al. (2024) shows that climate risk significantly worsens underinvestment and inefficient investment by increasing firms’ financial constraints and cash holdings. Moreover, government plays a key role in investment decisions, as industrial development and economic growth closely relate to policy guidance (Liu et al., 2022). Existing studies mainly evaluate the effects of specific policies. For example, environmental regulations influence firms’ green investments, with state-owned enterprises generally investing more due to a stronger focus on social welfare (Chemmanur et al., 2024). However, policy uncertainty notably increases the value of delaying investments. Gulen and Ion (2016) point out that this option value of waiting suppresses capital investment, particularly for firms facing high investment irreversibility. Mo and Liu (2023) provide evidence that climate policy uncertainty significantly obstructs firms’ high-risk digital transformation initiatives. In addition, Chang et al. (2024) find that investment decisions in energy firms are adversely influenced by climate policy uncertainty, but firms can mitigate these effects by optimizing investment decisions and implementing corporate social responsibility (CSR) strategies. Huang and Sun (2024) and Zhang et al. (2025) indicate the negative impact of climate policy uncertainty on corporate investment through lens of investment irreversibility or corporate governance. But they are constrained on the precision of measuring local policy risks with neglecting regional differences and lack a comprehensive analysis of the influencing mechanisms of climate policy uncertainty.
    35→Examining how regional climate policy uncertainty shapes corporate real investment and the channels through which it operates allows for a better evaluation of regional climate risks and the identification of suitable policy measures and firm-level strategies to mitigate their negative consequences. Prior research has showed that unclear government climate policies substantially increase firms’ difficulty in assessing prospects, leading them to delay or reduce investments while awaiting clearer policy signals (Fuss et al., 2008). Real investment, characterized by large capital requirements and strong irreversibility, is particularly sensitive to rising uncertainty, which raises firms’ financing difficulties and significantly suppresses investment willingness (Bernanke, 1983). Furthermore, increased uncertainty reduces firms’ operating income (Binz, 2022) and encourages higher cash holdings (Liu et al., 2021), significantly lowering tangible asset investment. Based on the above analysis, this study proposes the following hypothesis:
    36→H1: Climate policy uncertainty significantly inhibits corporate real investment.
    37→Rising climate policy uncertainty significantly increases the probability of project default, leading investors to adopt more cautious risk assessments and demand higher risk premiums to compensate for heightened policy risks. These premiums are reflected across various financing channels, including the cost of bank debt (Francis et al., 2014), bond financing, and equity risk premiums (Pástor and Veronesi, 2013). As financing costs rise, firms become less willing to invest in fixed assets, resulting in a decline in fixed asset investment rates (Gilchrist et al., 2014).
    38→At the same time, heightened uncertainty alters banks’ risk appetites. In response, banks tend to reduce their exposure by limiting credit to firms with higher operational risks (Dai and Zhang, 2023), thereby further tightening firms’ access to external financing. Additionally, uncertainty surrounding environmental disclosure requirements often leads firms to reduce the quantity and quality of information they disclose, exacerbating information asymmetry and increasing credit constraints (Chen et al., 2018). Given that external financing constitutes a vital source of funds for real investment, any increase in financing constraints can substantially influence firms’ investment choices. Firms with greater financial constraints tend to scale back investment more sharply during periods of elevated uncertainty (Campello et al., 2010). Based on the above analysis, this study proposes the following hypothesis:
    39→H2: Climate policy uncertainty inhibits corporate real investment by amplifying financing constraints.
    40→Firms’ cash flow positions are a critical determinant of their investment decisions (Gilchrist and Himmelberg, 1995). Firms facing constrained cash holdings or elevated cash flow volatility are more likely to pursue conservative financial policies, thereby curbing their investment appetite (Hirth and Viswanatha, 2011). Moreover, climate policy uncertainty directly and substantially affects firms’ operating revenues and cash flow positions. Binz (2022) finds that rising uncertainty substantially compresses corporate revenues. Ren et al. (2022) further demonstrate that increased climate policy uncertainty leads firms to incur additional coping costs, thereby reducing their free cash flows. In the face of uncertainty, companies often adopt a precautionary approach, boosting their cash holdings (Liu et al., 2021) or allocate more resources to liquid assets (Kim et al., 1998), in order to buffer against potential risks, which in turn results in a reduction in real investment. Based on the above analysis, this study proposes the following hypothesis:
    41→H3: Climate policy uncertainty inhibits corporate real investment by reducing corporate cash flows.

    43→3.1 Model specifications
    44→To alleviate concerns regarding reverse causality, the baseline specification incorporates a one-period lag of the key explanatory variable—CPU index. Specifically, the model employs the prior year’s CPU index to account for firms’ real investment decisions in the current year. Since climate policy formulation and implementation typically involve a time lag, and firms’ responses to policy changes are also delayed, the policy uncertainty measured in the preceding year is more likely to influence firms’ investment decisions in the current year. Introducing lagged variables helps alleviate reverse causality concerns and enhances the reliability of the estimation results. Following Kim and Kung (2017), this study incorporates firm fixed effects as well as year fixed effects to control for unobserved heterogeneity at both levels.

    60→3.2 Variable selection
    61→3.2.1 Dependent variable
    62→The dependent variable employed in this study is corporate real investment (Invest). Consistent with Kim and Kung (2017), Gulen and Ion (2016), real investment is measured as the ratio of cash paid for the purchase and construction of fixed assets, intangible assets, and other long-term assets to the firm’s total assets at the beginning of the fiscal year. This measure effectively captures the scale of real investment while considering asset levels based on decision-making, thereby enhancing comparability across firms of varying scales.
    63→3.2.2 Key explanatory variable
    64→The key explanatory variable in this study is the Climate Policy Uncertainty index measured at the city level. Ma et al. (2023) manually audited news articles from six major Chinese newspapers, including People’s Daily and Guangming Daily, to identify content related to climate policy uncertainty. They then trained a MacBERT-based machine learning model to classify the presence of such content in news texts. Using this model, they annotated a large dataset and calculated the number of relevant articles published each month. The monthly counts were subsequently standardized to construct the CPU Index. This methodology captures the introduction of major regulatory policies and shifts in policy tone, effectively addressing the subjectivity and semantic ambiguity associated with traditional approaches. Accordingly, the index provides a more reliable and granular measure of regional CPU.
    65→To mitigate the skewed distribution (Gulen and Ion, 2016), the original CPU index is taken to the natural logarithm. The city-level lnCPU index enables the identification of cross-regional variation in policy uncertainty. 
    66→3.2.3 Control variables
    67→In line with the existing literature on corporate real investment (Gulen and Ion, 2016; Kim and Kung, 2017; An et al., 2016), we construct the set of control variables by considering both firm-level characteristics and macro conditions. We include firm size (Size), financial leverage (Lev), operating profits growth rate (Growth), return on assets (ROA), the difference between the actual controller’s control rights and ownership rights (Separation), the total executive compensation (SumSalary), TobinQ, and the book-to-market ratio (BM). And we include personal income (PGDP) and economic growth (GGDP) at the city level. Detailed definitions of the primary variables are presented in Appendix 1.
    68→3.2.4 Mechanism variables
    69→To better understand how climate policy uncertainty affects firms’ real investment decisions, we further examine two potential channels: financing constraints and cash flow.
    70→Financing constraints capture the extent to which firms are limited in their ability to obtain external capital—an important factor influencing investment behavior. Due to information asymmetry, agency problems, and transaction costs, firms often face varying degrees of difficulty in accessing external financing (Doshi et al., 2018). When such constraints are more severe, firms may be forced to scale back investment, even when profitable opportunities exist. We measure financing constraints using the SA index (Hadlock and Pierce, 2010) and WW index (Whited and Wu, 2006).
    71→Cash flow (CF) serves as a key internal funding source for investment. Firms with healthier cash flow positions are generally more flexible in pursuing capital expenditures, whereas those with limited internal liquidity may adopt more cautious investment strategies (Hirth and Viswanatha, 2011). In our analysis, cash flow is measured as the ratio of net cash flow from operating activities to total assets at the beginning or end of the fiscal year (Gulen and Ion, 2016; Kim and Kung, 2017). 
    72→3.3 Data
    73→3.3.1 Sample
    74→The sample is constructed from all Chinese A-share listed firms between 2007 and 2023. We exclude firms under special treatment (ST, *ST, or PT), firms listed for less than one year, delisted or suspended firms, and those in the financial sector. Observations with missing values for key variables are also removed. To reduce the impact of outliers, all continuous variables are winsorized at the 1% level in both tails. After these procedures, the final sample comprises 29,026 firm-year observations from 3,563 distinct listed firms.
    75→Firm-level data are obtained from the China Stock Market and Accounting Research (CSMAR) database. The CPU index is sourced from Global Climate Risk Integration Database (GCRID) (Ma et al., 2023).

    86→	•	Robustness checks
    87→To ensure the reliability of the research findings, we conduct a series of robustness checks. All test results consistently support the conclusions derived from the baseline regressions.
    88→First, we add industry and province fixed effects separately to control other industrial and regional factors that affect firms’ investment. The regression results are reported in Columns (1) and (2) of Table 3. In both cases, the coefficient on lnCPU remains significantly negative, confirming the robustness of the baseline findings regarding the adverse impact of climate policy uncertainty on firms’ real investment.
    89→In addition, physical climate risks, like heavy rainfalls and rising sea level pose new challenges to firms’ operation, affects their financing costs and capital structure, further affect their investment (Ginglinger and Moreau, 2023). So, we introduce city-level climate physical risk index (CPRI) developed by (Guo et al., 2024) and take the logarithm to gain lnCPRI as a new control variable. Considering the well-documented impact of economic policy uncertainty on corporate investment decisions (Liu et al., 2022), we incorporate a firm-level measure of economic policy uncertainty perception (FEPU) as an additional control variable. This variable is constructed following the methodology of (Baker et al., 2016), by manually compiling lists of economic policy terms and uncertainty terms. Using Python-based web crawlers and the Jieba word segmentation tool, we identify sentences in corporate annual reports that simultaneously contain terms from both lists. The FEPU index is then calculated as the proportion of uncertainty-related terms within these sentences to the total word count. The regression results reported in Column (3) and (4) of Table 3 shows that the coefficient on CPU remains significantly negative after controlling for lnCPRI or FEPU, reinforcing the robustness of the main findings.
    90→To further address potential biases from major external shocks, we exclude observations from the 2010 global financial crisis and the 2020 COVID-19 pandemic. The regression results in Column (5) of Table 3 indicate that, even after removing these exceptional periods, the adverse effect of CPU on firms’ real investment remains statistically significant. This finding further corroborates our baseline findings.

    92→Although the core explanatory variable has been lagged by one period in the baseline regression, the model may still face endogeneity concerns stemming from two main sources: first, firms’ production activities can affect the environment, which in turn influences climate policy formulation, resulting in potential reverse causality; second, omitted variable bias may arise from unobserved factors that simultaneously impact both climate policy uncertainty and corporate real investment.
    93→To further address these endogeneity issues, this study uses the number of extreme high-temperature days (HTD) of a city, as an instrumental variable for climate policy uncertainty and applies a two-stage least squares (2SLS) regression approach. The rationale for this instrument is twofold: firstly, extreme weather events directly reflect climate change and embody physical climate risks. Since climate policies are formulated in response to climate change, an increase in extreme temperature days likely motivates policymakers to take action. Secondly, hot weather shocks are exogenous natural phenomena, unaffected by individual firms’ investment decisions. This instrument is not directly correlated with firms’ productivity and profitability (Addoum et al., 2020), further related to firm’s investment, but influences corporate investment only indirectly via climate policy formulation and firms’ real investment, thereby satisfying the exogeneity requirement.
    94→By introducing instrumental variable techniques, this approach mitigates reverse causality and reduces bias from omitted variables, yielding more reliable and robust empirical evidence on the effect of CPU on corporate real investment.
    95→Table 4 presents the results of the 2SLS regression. First, endogeneity tests indicate that CPU suffers from significant endogeneity, necessitating the use of instrumental variables for estimation. Second, the LM and F statistics both exceed the critical value of 10 by a large margin, indicating a strong association between the selected instruments and CPU, thus ruling out weak instrument concerns and ensuring model identification validity. In the first-stage regression (Column 1), the instrumental variable—extreme high temperature days (HTD)—is positively and significantly associated with lnCPU at the 1% level, indicating strong explanatory power. The second-stage regression results (Column 2) show that the instrumented lnCPU has a significantly negative effect on corporate physical investment, significant at the 10% level, further confirming that climate policy uncertainty substantially inhibits firms’ physical investment.

   100→The DID model requires the parallel trends assumption to hold. Following the approach of Beck et al. (2010) and Luo et al. (2025), this study applies truncation for samples far from the policy implementation date. Years after 2021 are treated as the +6 period, and years before 2009 are treated as the -6 period, with the year of 2015 as the baseline, to conduct a dynamic effect test for the parallel trend assumption. Figure 2 presents the results of the parallel trends test, where the estimated coefficients after the policy were demeaned. Before the policy implementation, the coefficients are near zero and the 90% confidence intervals include zero, suggesting no significant differences in both groups, thus satisfying the parallel trends assumption. After the policy, the investment levels of firms in the treatment group declined significantly compared to the control group, demonstrating the policy’s negative impact.

   102→To further validate the robustness of the findings, this study employs the PSM method to balance differences between the treatment and control groups. PSM is used before DID to reduce selection bias and ensure comparability within both groups, strengthening the parallel trends assumption and the credibility of causal estimates. Specifically, all control variables in the model are used to perform 1:1 nearest-neighbor matching on the cross-section, retaining only samples that have no-zero weight for the subsequent regression analysis. After PSM, Panel B of Table 5 indicates no statistically significant differences in the matching variables across groups. In Panel A, column (3) and (4) report regression results indicating that after PSM matching, the coefficient of the Treat*Time interaction term remains significantly negative at the 10% level. The result further confirms the inhibitory effect of CPU on firms’ tangible investment and strengthens the credibility of the baseline findings.

   104→The baseline regression reveals that heightened climate policy uncertainty significantly reduces firms' real investment. To further uncover the underlying mechanisms and validate our hypotheses, this study applies Model (2) to perform the mechanism analysis.
   105→The results are shown in Table 6. The regression results are after controlling for time and firm fixed effects, as well as other relevant variables. Specifically, a one-unit increase in CPU is associated with an 0.3% decrease in firms’ cash flow levels, 0.6% decrease in SA index and 0.2% increase in WW index.
   106→Additionally, external financing and internal cash flow are critical funding sources for corporate investment. And the exacerbation of information asymmetry and the increase in financing costs often lead to intensified financing constraints, which directly suppress firms’ investment propensity and reduce their investment levels (Campello et al., 2010; Gilchrist et al., 2014). Meanwhile, firms with poor cash flow conditions tend to adopt more conservative operational strategies, thereby weakening their investment willingness (Hirth and Viswanatha, 2011). 
   107→Collectively, these results suggest that the negative impact of CPU on real investment operates through both heightened financial constraints and reduced internal liquidity, supporting our hypothesis H2 and hypothesis H3.

   120→Besides, stable supply chain relationships are a prerequisite for steady business operations and a key source of stable cash flows for firms, especially under climate risks. Considering supply chains relationships, trade credit functions as a primary channel through which firms obtain short-term external financing within a short period, yet rising uncertainty increases cash flow volatility and default risk, leading firms to reduce trade credit (D’Mello and Toscano, 2020). Cao et al. (2024) find that climate-risk- driven uncertainty leads suppliers to raise their precautionary cash reserves, thereby significantly reducing trade credit provision, which further exacerbates financing constraints for customer firms and affects downstream firms’ operations and investment decisions.
   121→This paper further examines how trade credit conditions influence firms’ investment decisions under climate policy uncertainty. We measure bilateral trade credit using the ratio of accounts receivable to sales and the ratio of accounts payable to cost of goods sold. Based on the median values of these ratios, the sample is divided into high- and low-trade-credit groups for separate regressions. The regression results are reported in Table 10. Column (1) shows that when firms act as lenders, larger trade credit amplifies the adverse effect of CPU on firms’ real investment. In contrast, Column (4) indicates that when firms act as borrowers, greater trade credit alleviates the negative impact of CPU. These results suggest that supply chain financing can act as an effective mechanism for firms to mitigate the adverse impacts of CPU. By obtaining more trade credit from upstream suppliers and reducing liquidity transfers to downstream customers, firms can effectively ease financing constraints and cash flow pressures.

   123→In addition, customer and supplier concentration also influence firms’ financing constraints. Pankratz and Schiller (2024) show that when customers perceive suppliers’ climate risks to be greater than expected, they are more likely to switch suppliers, thereby reducing suppliers’ revenue and weakening supply chain stability. So, higher customer concentration can further amplify this negative effect, increase firms’ cash flow risk and significantly raise both the cost of equity capital and the cost of debt financing (Dhaliwal et al., 2016). Besides, large customers, with stronger bargaining power, can exert pressure on suppliers by lowering prices or delaying payments, thereby transferring their own operational risks to upstream firms (Ni et al., 2023). Besides, in the high disruption or uncertain stage, high customer concentration increases the risks of decreasing orders and uncollectable receivables, but the decreasing demand decreases firms’ dependence on suppliers, balance their power, and allow firms to access more resources and financing support from suppliers, making stable supplier relationships critical to promote firms’ resilience (Jiang et al., 2023).
   124→Based on this rationale, firms are classified into high- and low-concentration groups using the median values of customer and supplier concentration and conduct regressions to examine how concentration levels affect the impact of climate policy uncertainty. The regression results are reported in Table 11. Columns (1) and (4) indicate that the negative impact of CPU is stronger for firms with higher customer concentration and lower supplier concentration. This is because when companies become more reliant on key customers, those customers can shift risks upstream by reducing demand and delaying payments, intensifying cash flow pressures and ultimately impacting corporate investment decisions. However, companies with higher supplier concentration can deepen supplier relationships and secure greater credit support to address climate risks. This underscores the importance of balancing supply relationships to reduce operational risks and alleviate financial pressures amid climate policy uncertainty.

   126→	•	Conclusions
   127→With the increasing frequency of global climate extremes and growing international attention to climate change, governments across the globe have implemented a range of climate-related policies. However, uncertainties surrounding the timing, content, and intensity of these policies have significantly influenced firms’ operational and investment decisions. Using data from Chinese A-share listed companies between 2007 and 2023, this study finds that climate policy uncertainty significantly inhibits corporate physical investment, a result that is robust across various checks. This inhibitory effect operates primarily through two channels: amplifying financing constraints and reducing corporate cash flows. The negative impact of climate policy uncertainty is particularly pronounced for firms located in cities with slower economic growth, those operating in less competitive industries, private enterprises, firms in the manufacturing sector, and those in labor intensive industries. Notably, firms that actively adopt strong ESG practices are better able to withstand these adverse effects, underscoring the importance of proactive environmental strategies in managing policy-related risks. From the perspectives of supply chain ships, firms can mitigate the adverse effects of CPU by obtaining more trade credit from upstream suppliers, reducing liquidity transfers to downstream customers, lowering customer concentration and enhancing collaboration with suppliers.
   128→To mitigate the negative impact of CPU on corporate real investment, this study offers recommendations at three levels: policy design, government support, and corporate practices. First, climate policies should prioritize stability and transparency by maintaining consistency, adopting phased implementation, minimizing frequent revisions, and publicly disclosing policy details through channels such as white papers to help firms establish stable expectations. Second, governments should enhance financial support for firms, particularly in high climate exposure industries, by providing stable subsidies, tax incentives, and green credit programs, as well as establishing climate and green innovation funds to drive technological R&D and promote regional coordinated development. Third, firms should align with the climate and green policy by improving climate risk management, enhancing environmental and climate information disclosure, and adopting ESG principles. Manufacturing, in particular, should accelerate green production and technological transformation to better adapt to future policy changes. They should also build resilient supply chains, strengthen trade credit risks management, diversify their customers base, and cooperate with suppliers to address climate challenges. Through coordinated efforts between governments and firms, it is possible to mitigate the adverse effects of CPU, promote green transformation, and achieve sustainable economic development.
