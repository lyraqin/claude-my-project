   214→# Literature review and hypothesis development
   215→
   216→Climate change affects not only fundamental economic activities such as
   217→agricultural production and resource allocation (Kates et al., 1985) but
   218→also deeply influences societal organization, cultural characteristics,
   219→and political structures (Schneider et al., 2013). Its impacts permeate
   220→multiple levels of society and individual life, exhibiting broad,
   221→profound, and systemic features that shape government policymaking.
   222→Deryugina (2017) demonstrates that post-disaster government transfer
   223→payments increase substantially through a longitudinal analysis of
   224→hurricane events, with their present value far exceeding that of direct
   225→disaster aid. This sustained fiscal loosening poses risks to public
   226→finance sustainability, alters interregional resource distribution, and
   227→ultimately affects economic development in a significant manner.
   228→Moreover, Wang et al. (2024) find that robust climate policy frameworks
   229→contribute to lower macro-financial risks, as evidenced by notable
   230→decreases in debt and currency exposure in developed countries enforcing
   231→stringent climate regulations. The formulation and execution of climate
   232→policy is a complex, multidimensional process that demands a dynamic
   233→balance between economic growth and emission reduction goals, government
   234→intervention and market mechanisms, as well as national interests and
   235→regional disparities.
   236→
   237→In recent years, the frequency and intensity of extreme climate events
   238→have continued to rise, compounded by energy security crises triggered
   239→by escalating geopolitical conflicts and the accelerated advancement of
   240→global climate governance. Together, these factors create a highly
   241→uncertain policy environment that profoundly affects corporate
   242→development strategies (Ren et al., 2022) and energy consumption
   243→patterns (Gao et al., 2024). Through textual analysis, Gavriilidis
   244→(2021) develops an index capturing climate policy uncertainty, enabling
   245→a systematic assessment of its broad economic effects. Bouri et al.
   246→(2022) show that climate policy uncertainty significantly influences the
   247→comparative returns of green and brown energy equities, offering new
   248→perspectives for investment risk management and allocation strategies.
   249→Meanwhile, Gao et al. (2024) find that such uncertainty amplifies
   250→extreme risk transmissions between the broader economy and metal prices,
   251→with consequential effects on commodity market stability. For financial
   252→institutions, climate policy uncertainty significantly alters banks'
   253→risk preferences, reducing both active and passive risk-taking and
   254→increasing bankruptcy risk (Dai and Zhang, 2023). In the industrial
   255→economy, empirical evidence from (Lin, 2024) indicates that climate
   256→policy uncertainty substantially reduces urban energy efficiency,
   257→constraining industrial upgrading and energy transition. At the firm
   258→level, Ren et al. (2022) demonstrate that climate policy uncertainty
   259→lowers R&D investment and cash flow, significantly suppressing total
   260→factor productivity. Collectively, these studies underscore the
   261→potential threats of climate policy uncertainty to macroeconomic
   262→stability and financial markets, yet research focusing on its impact on
   263→corporate performance remains limited.
   264→
   265→On the other hand, while a growing body of research has examined how
   266→climate-related factors influence corporate investment decisions,
   267→research perspectives remain limited, mainly covering physical climate
   268→risks and climate policies. Xu et al. (2024) shows that climate risk
   269→significantly worsens underinvestment and inefficient investment by
   270→increasing firms' financial constraints and cash holdings. Moreover,
   271→government plays a key role in investment decisions, as industrial
   272→development and economic growth closely relate to policy guidance (Liu
   273→et al., 2022). Existing studies mainly evaluate the effects of specific
   274→policies. For example, environmental regulations influence firms' green
   275→investments, with state-owned enterprises generally investing more due
   276→to a stronger focus on social welfare (Chemmanur et al., 2024). However,
   277→policy uncertainty notably increases the value of delaying investments.
   278→Gulen and Ion (2016) point out that this option value of waiting
   279→suppresses capital investment, particularly for firms facing high
   280→investment irreversibility. Mo and Liu (2023) provide evidence that
   281→climate policy uncertainty significantly obstructs firms' high-risk
   282→digital transformation initiatives. In addition, Chang et al. (2024)
   283→find that investment decisions in energy firms are adversely influenced
   284→by climate policy uncertainty, but firms can mitigate these effects by
   285→optimizing investment decisions and implementing corporate social
   286→responsibility (CSR) strategies. [Huang and Sun (2024) and Zhang et al.
   287→(2025) indicate the negative impact of climate policy uncertainty on
   288→corporate investment through lens of investment irreversibility or
   289→corporate governance. But they are constrained on the precision of
   290→measuring local policy risks with neglecting regional differences and
   291→lack a comprehensive analysis of the influencing mechanisms of climate
   292→policy uncertainty.]{.mark}
   293→
   294→Examining how regional climate policy uncertainty shapes corporate real
   295→investment and the channels through which it operates allows for a
   296→better evaluation of regional climate risks and the identification of
   297→suitable policy measures and firm-level strategies to mitigate their
   298→negative consequences. Prior research has showed that unclear government
   299→climate policies substantially increase firms' difficulty in assessing
   300→prospects, leading them to delay or reduce investments while awaiting
   301→clearer policy signals (Fuss et al., 2008). Real investment,
   302→characterized by large capital requirements and strong irreversibility,
   303→is particularly sensitive to rising uncertainty, which raises firms'
   304→financing difficulties and significantly suppresses investment
   305→willingness (Bernanke, 1983). Furthermore, increased uncertainty reduces
   306→firms' operating income (Binz, 2022) and encourages higher cash holdings
   307→(Liu et al., 2021), significantly lowering tangible asset investment.
   308→Based on the above analysis, this study proposes the following
   309→hypothesis:
   310→
   311→***H1: Climate policy uncertainty significantly inhibits corporate real
   312→investment.***
   313→
   314→Rising climate policy uncertainty significantly increases the
   315→probability of project default, leading investors to adopt more cautious
   316→risk assessments and demand higher risk premiums to compensate for
   317→heightened policy risks. These premiums are reflected across various
   318→financing channels, including the cost of bank debt (Francis et al.,
   319→2014), bond financing, and equity risk premiums (Pástor and Veronesi,
   320→2013). As financing costs rise, firms become less willing to invest in
   321→fixed assets, resulting in a decline in fixed asset investment rates
   322→(Gilchrist et al., 2014).
   323→
   324→At the same time, heightened uncertainty alters banks' risk appetites.
   325→In response, banks tend to reduce their exposure by limiting credit to
   326→firms with higher operational risks (Dai and Zhang, 2023), thereby
   327→further tightening firms' access to external financing. Additionally,
   328→uncertainty surrounding environmental disclosure requirements often
   329→leads firms to reduce the quantity and quality of information they
   330→disclose, exacerbating information asymmetry and increasing credit
   331→constraints (Chen et al., 2018). Given that external financing
   332→constitutes a vital source of funds for real investment, any increase in
   333→financing constraints can substantially influence firms' investment
   334→choices. Firms with greater financial constraints tend to scale back
   335→investment more sharply during periods of elevated uncertainty (Campello
   336→et al., 2010). Based on the above analysis, this study proposes the
   337→following hypothesis:
   338→
   339→***H2: Climate policy uncertainty inhibits corporate real investment by
   340→amplifying financing constraints.***
   341→
   342→Firms' cash flow positions are a critical determinant of their
   343→investment decisions (Gilchrist and Himmelberg, 1995). Firms facing
   344→constrained cash holdings or elevated cash flow volatility are more
   345→likely to pursue conservative financial policies, thereby curbing their
   346→investment appetite (Hirth and Viswanatha, 2011). Moreover, climate
   347→policy uncertainty directly and substantially affects firms' operating
   348→revenues and cash flow positions. Binz (2022) finds that rising
   349→uncertainty substantially compresses corporate revenues. Ren et al.
   350→(2022) further demonstrate that increased climate policy uncertainty
   351→leads firms to incur additional coping costs, thereby reducing their
   352→free cash flows. In the face of uncertainty, companies often adopt a
   353→precautionary approach, boosting their cash holdings (Liu et al., 2021)
   354→or allocate more resources to liquid assets (Kim et al., 1998), in order
   355→to buffer against potential risks, which in turn results in a reduction
   356→in real investment. Based on the above analysis, this study proposes the
   357→following hypothesis:
   358→
   359→***H3: Climate policy uncertainty inhibits corporate real investment by
   360→reducing corporate cash flows.***

   445→## 3.2 Variable selection
   446→
   447→### 3.2.1 Dependent variable
   448→
   449→The dependent variable employed in this study is corporate real
   450→investment (*Invest*). Consistent with Kim and Kung (2017), Gulen and
   451→Ion (2016), real investment is measured as the ratio of cash paid for
   452→the purchase and construction of fixed assets, intangible assets, and
   453→other long-term assets to the firm's total assets at the beginning of
   454→the fiscal year. This measure effectively captures the scale of real
   455→investment while considering asset levels based on decision-making,
   456→thereby enhancing comparability across firms of varying scales.
   457→
   458→### 3.2.2 Key explanatory variable
   459→
   460→The key explanatory variable in this study is the Climate Policy
   461→Uncertainty index measured at the city level. Ma et al. (2023) manually
   462→audited news articles from six major Chinese newspapers, including
   463→*People's Daily* and *Guangming Daily*, to identify content related to
   464→climate policy uncertainty. They then trained a MacBERT-based machine
   465→learning model to classify the presence of such content in news texts.
   466→Using this model, they annotated a large dataset and calculated the
   467→number of relevant articles published each month. The monthly counts
   468→were subsequently standardized to construct the CPU Index. This
   469→methodology captures the introduction of major regulatory policies and
   470→shifts in policy tone, effectively addressing the subjectivity and
   471→semantic ambiguity associated with traditional approaches. Accordingly,
   472→the index provides a more reliable and granular measure of regional CPU.
   473→
   474→[To mitigate the skewed distribution (Gulen and Ion, 2016), the original
   475→CPU index is taken to the natural logarithm]{.mark}. The city-level
   476→lnCPU index enables the identification of cross-regional variation in
   477→policy uncertainty.

   493→### 3.2.4 Mechanism variables
   494→
   495→To better understand how climate policy uncertainty affects firms' real
   496→investment decisions, we further examine two potential channels:
   497→financing constraints and cash flow.
   498→
   499→Financing constraints capture the extent to which firms are limited in
   500→their ability to obtain external capital---an important factor
   501→influencing investment behavior. Due to information asymmetry, agency
   502→problems, and transaction costs, firms often face varying degrees of
   503→difficulty in accessing external financing (Doshi et al., 2018). When
   504→such constraints are more severe, firms may be forced to scale back
   505→investment, even when profitable opportunities exist. [We measure
   506→financing constraints using the SA index (Hadlock and Pierce, 2010) and
   507→WW index (Whited and Wu, 2006)]{.mark}.
   508→
   509→Cash flow (*CF*) serves as a key internal funding source for investment.
   510→Firms with healthier cash flow positions are generally more flexible in
   511→pursuing capital expenditures, whereas those with limited internal
   512→liquidity may adopt more cautious investment strategies (Hirth and
   513→Viswanatha, 2011). In our analysis, cash flow is measured as the ratio
   514→of net cash flow from operating activities to total assets at the
   515→beginning or end of the fiscal year (Gulen and Ion, 2016; Kim and Kung,
   516→2017).

   935→# Conclusions
   936→
   937→With the increasing frequency of global climate extremes and growing
   938→international attention to climate change, governments across the globe
   939→have implemented a range of climate-related policies. However,
   940→uncertainties surrounding the timing, content, and intensity of these
   941→policies have significantly influenced firms' operational and investment
   942→decisions. Using data from Chinese A-share listed companies between 2007
   943→and 2023, this study finds that climate policy uncertainty significantly
   944→inhibits corporate physical investment, a result that is robust across
   945→various checks. This inhibitory effect operates primarily through two
   946→channels: amplifying financing constraints and reducing corporate cash
   947→flows. The negative impact of climate policy uncertainty is particularly
   948→pronounced for firms located in cities with slower economic growth,
   949→those operating in less competitive industries, private enterprises,
   950→firms in the manufacturing sector, and those in labor intensive
   951→industries. Notably, firms that actively adopt strong ESG practices are
   952→better able to withstand these adverse effects, underscoring the
   953→importance of proactive environmental strategies in managing
   954→policy-related risks. From the perspectives of supply chain ships, firms
   955→can mitigate the adverse effects of CPU by obtaining more trade credit
   956→from upstream suppliers, reducing liquidity transfers to downstream
   957→customers, lowering customer concentration and enhancing collaboration
   958→with suppliers.
   959→
   960→To mitigate the negative impact of CPU on corporate real investment,
   961→this study offers recommendations at three levels: policy design,
   962→government support, and corporate practices. First, climate policies
   963→should prioritize stability and transparency by maintaining consistency,
   964→adopting phased implementation, minimizing frequent revisions, and
   965→publicly disclosing policy details through channels such as white papers
   966→to help firms establish stable expectations. Second, governments should
   967→enhance financial support for firms, particularly in high climate
   968→exposure industries, by providing stable subsidies, tax incentives, and
   969→green credit programs, as well as establishing climate and green
   970→innovation funds to drive technological R&D and promote regional
   971→coordinated development. Third, firms should align with the climate and
   972→green policy by improving climate risk management, enhancing
   973→environmental and climate information disclosure, and adopting ESG
   974→principles. Manufacturing, in particular, should accelerate green
   975→production and technological transformation to better adapt to future
   976→policy changes. They should also build resilient supply chains,
   977→strengthen trade credit risks management, diversify their customers
   978→base, and cooperate with suppliers to address climate challenges.
   979→Through coordinated efforts between governments and firms, it is
   980→possible to mitigate the adverse effects of CPU, promote green
   981→transformation, and achieve sustainable economic development.
