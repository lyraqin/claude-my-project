   215→# Literature review and hypothesis development
   216→
   217→Climate change affects not only fundamental economic activities such as
   218→agricultural production and resource allocation (Kates et al., 1985) but
   219→also deeply influences societal organization, cultural characteristics,
   220→and political structures (Schneider et al., 2013). Its impacts permeate
   221→multiple levels of society and individual life, exhibiting broad,
   222→profound, and systemic features that shape government policymaking.
   223→Deryugina (2017) demonstrates that post-disaster government transfer
   224→payments increase substantially through a longitudinal analysis of
   225→hurricane events, with their present value far exceeding that of direct
   226→disaster aid. This sustained fiscal loosening poses risks to public
   227→finance sustainability, alters interregional resource distribution, and
   228→ultimately affects economic development in a significant manner.
   229→Moreover, Wang et al. (2024) find that robust climate policy frameworks
   230→contribute to lower macro-financial risks, as evidenced by notable
   231→decreases in debt and currency exposure in developed countries enforcing
   232→stringent climate regulations. The formulation and execution of climate
   233→policy is a complex, multidimensional process that demands a dynamic
   234→balance between economic growth and emission reduction goals, government
   235→intervention and market mechanisms, as well as national interests and
   236→regional disparities.
   237→
   238→In recent years, the frequency and intensity of extreme climate events
   239→have continued to rise, compounded by energy security crises triggered
   240→by escalating geopolitical conflicts and the accelerated advancement of
   241→global climate governance. Together, these factors create a highly
   242→uncertain policy environment that profoundly affects corporate
   243→development strategies (Ren et al., 2022) and energy consumption
   244→patterns (Gao et al., 2024). Through textual analysis, Gavriilidis
   245→(2021) develops an index capturing climate policy uncertainty, enabling
   246→a systematic assessment of its broad economic effects. Bouri et al.
   247→(2022) show that climate policy uncertainty significantly influences the
   248→comparative returns of green and brown energy equities, offering new
   249→perspectives for investment risk management and allocation strategies.
   250→Meanwhile, Gao et al. (2024) find that such uncertainty amplifies
   251→extreme risk transmissions between the broader economy and metal prices,
   252→with consequential effects on commodity market stability. For financial
   253→institutions, climate policy uncertainty significantly alters banks'
   254→risk preferences, reducing both active and passive risk-taking and
   255→increasing bankruptcy risk (Dai and Zhang, 2023). In the industrial
   256→economy, empirical evidence from (Lin, 2024) indicates that climate
   257→policy uncertainty substantially reduces urban energy efficiency,
   258→constraining industrial upgrading and energy transition. At the firm
   259→level, Ren et al. (2022) demonstrate that climate policy uncertainty
   260→lowers R&D investment and cash flow, significantly suppressing total
   261→factor productivity. Collectively, these studies underscore the
   262→potential threats of climate policy uncertainty to macroeconomic
   263→stability and financial markets, yet research focusing on its impact on
   264→corporate performance remains limited.
   265→
   266→On the other hand, while a growing body of research has examined how
   267→climate-related factors influence corporate investment decisions,
   268→research perspectives remain limited, mainly covering physical climate
   269→risks and climate policies. Xu et al. (2024) shows that climate risk
   270→significantly worsens underinvestment and inefficient investment by
   271→increasing firms' financial constraints and cash holdings. Moreover,
   272→government plays a key role in investment decisions, as industrial
   273→development and economic growth closely relate to policy guidance (Liu
   274→et al., 2022). Existing studies mainly evaluate the effects of specific
   275→policies. For example, environmental regulations influence firms' green
   276→investments, with state-owned enterprises generally investing more due
   277→to a stronger focus on social welfare (Chemmanur et al., 2024). However,
   278→policy uncertainty notably increases the value of delaying investments.
   279→Gulen and Ion (2016) point out that this option value of waiting
   280→suppresses capital investment, particularly for firms facing high
   281→investment irreversibility. Mo and Liu (2023) provide evidence that
   282→climate policy uncertainty significantly obstructs firms' high-risk
   283→digital transformation initiatives. In addition, Chang et al. (2024)
   284→find that investment decisions in energy firms are adversely influenced
   285→by climate policy uncertainty, but firms can mitigate these effects by
   286→optimizing investment decisions and implementing corporate social
   287→responsibility (CSR) strategies. [Huang and Sun (2024) and Zhang et al.
   288→(2025) indicate the negative impact of climate policy uncertainty on
   289→corporate investment through lens of investment irreversibility or
   290→corporate governance. But they are constrained on the precision of
   291→measuring local policy risks with neglecting regional differences and
   292→lack a comprehensive analysis of the influencing mechanisms of climate
   293→policy uncertainty.]{.mark}
   294→
   295→Examining how regional climate policy uncertainty shapes corporate real
   296→investment and the channels through which it operates allows for a
   297→better evaluation of regional climate risks and the identification of
   298→suitable policy measures and firm-level strategies to mitigate their
   299→negative consequences. Prior research has showed that unclear government
   300→climate policies substantially increase firms' difficulty in assessing
   301→prospects, leading them to delay or reduce investments while awaiting
   302→clearer policy signals (Fuss et al., 2008). Real investment,
   303→characterized by large capital requirements and strong irreversibility,
   304→is particularly sensitive to rising uncertainty, which raises firms'
   305→financing difficulties and significantly suppresses investment
   306→willingness (Bernanke, 1983). Furthermore, increased uncertainty reduces
   307→firms' operating income (Binz, 2022) and encourages higher cash holdings
   308→(Liu et al., 2021), significantly lowering tangible asset investment.
   309→Based on the above analysis, this study proposes the following
   310→hypothesis:
   311→
   312→***H1: Climate policy uncertainty significantly inhibits corporate real
   313→investment.***
   314→
   315→Rising climate policy uncertainty significantly increases the
   316→probability of project default, leading investors to adopt more cautious
   317→risk assessments and demand higher risk premiums to compensate for
   318→heightened policy risks. These premiums are reflected across various
   319→financing channels, including the cost of bank debt (Francis et al.,
   320→2014), bond financing, and equity risk premiums (Pástor and Veronesi,
   321→2013). As financing costs rise, firms become less willing to invest in
   322→fixed assets, resulting in a decline in fixed asset investment rates
   323→(Gilchrist et al., 2014).
   324→
   325→At the same time, heightened uncertainty alters banks' risk appetites.
   326→In response, banks tend to reduce their exposure by limiting credit to
   327→firms with higher operational risks (Dai and Zhang, 2023), thereby
   328→further tightening firms' access to external financing. Additionally,
   329→uncertainty surrounding environmental disclosure requirements often
   330→leads firms to reduce the quantity and quality of information they
   331→disclose, exacerbating information asymmetry and increasing credit
   332→constraints (Chen et al., 2018). Given that external financing
   333→constitutes a vital source of funds for real investment, any increase in
   334→financing constraints can substantially influence firms' investment
   335→choices. Firms with greater financial constraints tend to scale back
   336→investment more sharply during periods of elevated uncertainty (Campello
   337→et al., 2010). Based on the above analysis, this study proposes the
   338→following hypothesis:
   339→
   340→***H2: Climate policy uncertainty inhibits corporate real investment by
   341→amplifying financing constraints.***
   342→
   343→Firms' cash flow positions are a critical determinant of their
   344→investment decisions (Gilchrist and Himmelberg, 1995). Firms facing
   345→constrained cash holdings or elevated cash flow volatility are more
   346→likely to pursue conservative financial policies, thereby curbing their
   347→investment appetite (Hirth and Viswanatha, 2011). Moreover, climate
   348→policy uncertainty directly and substantially affects firms' operating
   349→revenues and cash flow positions. Binz (2022) finds that rising
   350→uncertainty substantially compresses corporate revenues. Ren et al.
   351→(2022) further demonstrate that increased climate policy uncertainty
   352→leads firms to incur additional coping costs, thereby reducing their
   353→free cash flows. In the face of uncertainty, companies often adopt a
   354→precautionary approach, boosting their cash holdings (Liu et al., 2021)
   355→or allocate more resources to liquid assets (Kim et al., 1998), in order
   356→to buffer against potential risks, which in turn results in a reduction
   357→in real investment. Based on the above analysis, this study proposes the
   358→following hypothesis:
   359→
   360→***H3: Climate policy uncertainty inhibits corporate real investment by
   361→reducing corporate cash flows.***

   446→## 3.2 Variable selection
   447→
   448→### 3.2.1 Dependent variable
   449→
   450→The dependent variable employed in this study is corporate real
   451→investment (*Invest*). Consistent with Kim and Kung (2017), Gulen and
   452→Ion (2016), real investment is measured as the ratio of cash paid for
   453→the purchase and construction of fixed assets, intangible assets, and
   454→other long-term assets to the firm's total assets at the beginning of
   455→the fiscal year. This measure effectively captures the scale of real
   456→investment while considering asset levels based on decision-making,
   457→thereby enhancing comparability across firms of varying scales.
   458→
   459→### 3.2.2 Key explanatory variable
   460→
   461→The key explanatory variable in this study is the Climate Policy
   462→Uncertainty index measured at the city level. Ma et al. (2023) manually
   463→audited news articles from six major Chinese newspapers, including
   464→*People's Daily* and *Guangming Daily*, to identify content related to
   465→climate policy uncertainty. They then trained a MacBERT-based machine
   466→learning model to classify the presence of such content in news texts.
   467→Using this model, they annotated a large dataset and calculated the
   468→number of relevant articles published each month. The monthly counts
   469→were subsequently standardized to construct the CPU Index. This
   470→methodology captures the introduction of major regulatory policies and
   471→shifts in policy tone, effectively addressing the subjectivity and
   472→semantic ambiguity associated with traditional approaches. Accordingly,
   473→the index provides a more reliable and granular measure of regional CPU.
   474→
   475→[To mitigate the skewed distribution (Gulen and Ion, 2016), the original
   476→CPU index is taken to the natural logarithm]{.mark}. The city-level
   477→lnCPU index enables the identification of cross-regional variation in
   478→policy uncertainty.

   494→### 3.2.4 Mechanism variables
   495→
   496→To better understand how climate policy uncertainty affects firms' real
   497→investment decisions, we further examine two potential channels:
   498→financing constraints and cash flow.
   499→
   500→Financing constraints capture the extent to which firms are limited in
   501→their ability to obtain external capital---an important factor
   502→influencing investment behavior. Due to information asymmetry, agency
   503→problems, and transaction costs, firms often face varying degrees of
   504→difficulty in accessing external financing (Doshi et al., 2018). When
   505→such constraints are more severe, firms may be forced to scale back
   506→investment, even when profitable opportunities exist. [We measure
   507→financing constraints using the SA index (Hadlock and Pierce, 2010) and
   508→WW index (Whited and Wu, 2006)]{.mark}.
   509→
